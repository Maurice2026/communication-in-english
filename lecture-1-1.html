Section I

Introduction: Sound vs. Script

The first hurdle in mastering English phonetics is recognising that English is not a phonetic language. As Roach (2022, p. 4) observes, the English writing system is historically irregular — the same sound can be spelled in multiple ways, and the same spelling can represent different sounds depending on context. Unlike some indigenous Nigerian languages where one letter consistently represents one sound, English frequently uses different letter combinations for the same sound (Gut, 2019, p. 98).

Consider these three words: rough, physics, fan. They look nothing alike — yet the letters gh, ph, and f all produce exactly the same sound: /f/. This fundamental mismatch between script and sound is the central challenge of English phonetics for learners in Nigerian tertiary institutions (Oyebola & Gut, 2020, p. 166).

Key Concepts
Orthography
The spelling system — the letters seen on the page. English orthography is notoriously irregular and cannot be relied upon as a guide to pronunciation (Davenport & Hannahs, 2020, p. 3).
Phonology
The sound system — the actual phonemes heard and produced in spoken language. Phonology studies how sounds function and pattern within a language (Skandera & Burleigh, 2019, p. 11).
Phoneme
The smallest unit of sound that distinguishes one word from another. Changing one phoneme changes the meaning: cat → bat → hat (Cruttenden, 2021, p. 18).
📌

The Golden Rule: In this course, we listen to what we hear, not what we see. The written word is a guide — the spoken sound is the truth.

Section II

The 44 Phonemes of English

The 26 Letters of the English Alphabet

The English alphabet contains 26 letters. These are the graphemes — the written symbols — from which all English words are constructed (Ladefoged & Johnson, 2020, p. 2):

ABCDEFGHIJKLMNOPQRSTUVWXYZ

Despite having only 26 letters, English has 44 distinct sounds — phonemes. This gap of 18 between letters and sounds explains why English spelling so often misleads the learner (Roach, 2022, p. 9). These 44 phonemes divide into 20 vowel sounds and 24 consonant sounds (Cruttenden, 2021, p. 22).

The 20 Vowel Sounds

Vowels are produced with an open vocal tract — the airstream from the lungs passes through the mouth without obstruction or narrowing sufficient to cause friction (Skandera & Burleigh, 2019, p. 25). English has 20 vowel sounds: 12 monophthongs and 8 diphthongs.

Diphthongs are gliding vowels in which the tongue moves from one position to another within the same syllable (Ladefoged & Johnson, 2020, p. 21).

The 24 Consonant Sounds

Consonants are produced with some degree of closure or narrowing in the vocal tract that obstructs or impedes the airstream (Davenport & Hannahs, 2020, p. 19). The key distinction between consonants is voicing — whether the vocal cords vibrate during production. Voiced consonants cause a buzz felt at the throat; voiceless ones produce only a rush of air (Roach, 2022, p. 31).

Section III

The Mechanics of Speech — Organs of Speech

To produce the 44 phonemes, the human body employs a coordinated system of organs sometimes collectively referred to as the vocal tract. Understanding this mechanism helps learners identify and correct first-language interference — the tendency to superimpose the phonological habits of one's mother tongue onto English (Oyebola & Gut, 2020, p. 170). As Ladefoged and Johnson (2020, p. 7) note, conscious awareness of articulator position is the first step toward deliberate self-correction.

Figure 1: Cross-section of the human vocal tract showing the principal organs involved in speech production.
Adapted from Ladefoged & Johnson (2020, p. 8) and Cruttenden (2021, p. 6).

Each organ plays a specific and critical role. The lungs supply the airstream that powers all speech sounds (Skandera & Burleigh, 2019, p. 16). The vocal cords in the larynx vibrate to produce voiced sounds and remain apart for voiceless ones. The tongue — the most mobile articulator — shapes vowels by adjusting its height and position, and forms consonants by contacting or approaching the palate, alveolar ridge, or teeth (Roach, 2022, p. 18). The lips are essential for bilabial sounds such as /p/, /b/, and /m/, while the teeth are necessary for dental fricatives: /θ/ as in thin and /ð/ as in this — sounds that frequently pose difficulties for Nigerian students due to first-language interference (Gut, 2019, p. 105).

Academic Note — First Language Interference
What it is
The unconscious application of first-language phonological rules to English — for example, substituting /t/ for /θ/, producing "tin" instead of "thin" (Aremo, 2021, p. 515).
Why it matters
It affects intelligibility in academic and professional settings. The goal is not to erase linguistic identity, but to expand the learner's phonological range so they can be clearly understood in any formal context (Deterding, 2018, p. 320; Jenkins, 2022, p. 8).
Section IV

Practical Activity: The Silent Letter Audit

One of the most direct demonstrations that English orthography does not reliably represent its phonology is the existence of silent letters — graphemes that appear in the spelling of a word but contribute no sound to its pronunciation (Davenport & Hannahs, 2020, p. 5). Silent letters are largely historical relics — remnants of earlier pronunciations or borrowings from Latin, Greek, and French that were retained in spelling long after the sounds disappeared from speech (Cruttenden, 2021, p. 20).

Section V

Conclusion & Key Takeaway

Mastering the 44 sounds is the first and most critical step toward International Intelligibility. As Jenkins (2022, p. 14) argues, intelligibility — not native-like accent — is the appropriate target for speakers of English as an additional language. The goal of this course is not to make the student sound British or American, but to ensure their speech is sufficiently clear to be understood by any English speaker, regardless of background (Deterding, 2018, p. 329).


A student who understands the relationship between the 26 letters and the 44 sounds — who knows why the p in psychology is silent, why rough ends with an /f/, and where the tongue must be placed to produce /θ/ — has established the phonological foundation on which all further language work in this course will build (Aremo, 2021, p. 522).
