Section I

Introduction: Why Reading Strategy Matters

Reading is the most frequently demanded academic skill at the tertiary level. Undergraduate students are expected to engage with textbooks, journal articles, reports, policy documents, and examination questions — each requiring a different mode of engagement with text (Grabe & Stoller, 2020, p. 3). Yet reading is not a single, uniform activity. It is a purposeful, strategic process in which the reader actively constructs meaning from text rather than passively receiving information (Anderson, 2018, p. 12).

Research consistently shows that students who read strategically — who choose their reading approach based on their purpose — outperform those who read everything at the same pace and depth (Mokhtari & Reichard, 2019, p. 54). This lecture introduces the three core reading strategies and the SQ3R technique, which are the tools of strategic academic reading.

Key Concept
Strategic Reading
The deliberate selection and application of reading techniques based on the reader's purpose, the nature of the text, and the demands of the task. Strategic readers adjust their approach continuously as they engage with a text (Grabe & Stoller, 2020, p. 8).
Reading Purpose
The goal the reader brings to a text — locating specific information, getting a general overview, deep comprehension, or critical evaluation. Purpose determines strategy (Anderson, 2018, p. 15).
Section II

Three Core Reading Strategies

Three fundamental reading strategies form the foundation of academic reading competence. Each serves a distinct purpose and is appropriate in different reading situations (Nuttall, 2020, p. 38).

⚡ Skimming

Purpose: To get the gist — a rapid overview of a text's main ideas without reading every word.

How: Read titles, headings, subheadings, the first and last sentence of each paragraph, topic sentences, and any bold or italicised text (Nuttall, 2020, p. 40).

When to use: Before deciding whether a text is worth reading in full · Previewing a chapter before a lecture · Getting a quick overview of a long report.

🔍 Scanning

Purpose: To locate specific information within a text — a particular date, name, statistic, or definition.

How: Move your eyes rapidly across the page looking for key words, numbers, or patterns that signal the target information. Do not read surrounding text unless the target is found (Grabe & Stoller, 2020, p. 22).

When to use: Finding a definition in a textbook · Locating a citation in a reference list · Answering specific examination questions.

📖 Intensive Reading

Purpose: Deep, detailed comprehension of a text — understanding every sentence, argument, and implication.

How: Read slowly and carefully, pausing to note unfamiliar vocabulary, annotate key arguments, and question the author's reasoning (Anderson, 2018, p. 28).

When to use: Reading a core text for an essay · Analysing a journal article for a literature review · Preparing for a seminar discussion.

🎯 Extensive Reading

Purpose: Building fluency, vocabulary, and background knowledge through reading large quantities of text for general comprehension and enjoyment.

How: Choose texts slightly below your maximum comprehension level. Read for pleasure and meaning rather than linguistic analysis (Day & Bamford, 2022, p. 7).

When to use: Building general knowledge in a subject area · Improving reading speed and fluency · Broadening academic vocabulary over time.

📌

Strategic Combination: Expert readers combine strategies in sequence. A typical approach to a new academic text: Skim first to assess relevance → Scan for key terms → Read intensively the most relevant sections. Never begin with intensive reading before you know the text is worth the investment of time (Mokhtari & Reichard, 2019, p. 60).

Section III

The SQ3R Technique

The SQ3R technique is a structured, five-stage reading method developed for academic study. It transforms passive reading into active learning by requiring the reader to engage with text before, during, and after reading. Research demonstrates that students who apply SQ3R consistently achieve significantly higher comprehension and retention than those who read linearly (Artis, 2018, p. 82).

LetterStageWhat to DoWhy It Works
S Survey Skim the entire text before reading. Read the title, abstract or introduction, headings, subheadings, figures, captions, and the conclusion or summary (Artis, 2018, p. 83). Activates prior knowledge. Creates a mental framework — a "map" of the text — before detailed reading begins.
Q Question Convert each heading and subheading into a question before reading that section. Write the questions down (Nuttall, 2020, p. 55). Gives each section a specific reading purpose. The brain reads more actively when searching for an answer to a specific question.
R¹ Read Read each section carefully to find answers to your questions. Do not highlight indiscriminately — note only information that directly answers your questions (Anderson, 2018, p. 35). Focused reading with a clear purpose prevents passive, unfocused engagement with the text.
R² Recite After reading each section, close the text and recite — aloud or in writing — the answer to your question in your own words (Artis, 2018, p. 85). Recitation is the most powerful memory consolidation strategy. If you cannot recite it, you have not yet understood it.
R³ Review After completing the full text, review your notes and questions. Identify gaps in understanding. Re-read only the sections you could not recite accurately (Nuttall, 2020, p. 58). Review strengthens long-term retention and identifies exactly which parts of the text require further engagement.
Section IV

Reading for Academic Purposes

Academic reading differs from general reading in several critical ways. Academic texts are denser, more technical, and more argumentatively structured than general texts. They presuppose background knowledge, use specialised vocabulary, and require the reader to evaluate arguments rather than merely absorb information (Grabe & Stoller, 2020, p. 45).

Skills for Academic Reading
Identifying the Main Argument
Every academic text has a central claim or thesis. Locating this — usually in the introduction and conclusion — gives the reader a framework for evaluating all other content (Cottrell, 2019, p. 68).
Distinguishing Evidence from Claim
Academic writers support claims with evidence — data, quotations, examples. A critical reader must ask: what is the claim, and what evidence supports it? Are the two logically connected? (Anderson, 2018, p. 42).
Using Context Clues
When encountering unfamiliar vocabulary, skilled readers use the surrounding text — definitions embedded in the sentence, contrast signals like however, or example signals like such as — to infer meaning without stopping to consult a dictionary (Nuttall, 2020, p. 72).
Critical Reading
Beyond comprehension, academic reading requires evaluation: Is the argument logical? Are the sources credible? Does the evidence actually support the conclusion? These are the questions of a critical reader (Cottrell, 2019, p. 74).
Section V

Practical Activity: Apply the Strategy

The ten items below test your ability to match reading purpose with strategy, apply SQ3R stages, and demonstrate critical reading awareness (Mokhtari & Reichard, 2019, p. 65).

✏️
Practical Activity
Reading Strategy Application — 10 Items

For each scenario or question below, identify the most appropriate reading strategy or SQ3R stage, and write a one-sentence justification for your answer.

  • 1
    You have 10 minutes before a lecture. A 30-page chapter has been assigned. What do you do?
    Which strategy gives the best overview in limited time?
    Skimming
  • 2
    You need the publication date of a study mentioned in a textbook. How do you find it?
    You are searching for one specific piece of data.
    Scanning
  • 3
    Your essay requires a detailed analysis of a journal article's argument. How do you read it?
    You need full comprehension of every section.
    Intensive Reading
  • 4
    In SQ3R, what should you do before reading a section titled "The Causes of Inflation"?
    The heading must be converted into a question.
    Q — Question
  • 5
    After completing a section, you close the book and try to explain the content in your own words. Which SQ3R stage is this?
    Testing yourself without looking at the text.
    R² — Recite
  • 6
    You read the abstract, headings, and conclusion of a new article before beginning. Which SQ3R stage is this?
    Getting a global view of the text before committing to it.
    S — Survey
  • 7
    The sentence reads: "The policy was draconian — so severe that it alienated even its supporters." What is the meaning of draconian?
    Use the context clue after the dash.
    Context Clue
  • 8
    A student reads every word of every text assigned at the same slow pace. What is the problem?
    Identify the missing element of strategic reading.
    No Strategy
  • 9
    After completing an entire text, you go back to check your questions against your notes and re-read unclear sections. Which SQ3R stage?
    The final consolidation and gap-filling stage.
    R³ — Review
  • 10
    A critical reader asks: "Does the evidence actually support the author's conclusion?" What reading level does this represent?
    Beyond comprehension — what level is this?
    Critical Reading
Section VI

Conclusion & Key Takeaway

Reading is not a passive act of receiving information — it is an active, purposeful, and strategic engagement with text. A student who skims before reading intensively, who converts headings into questions before reading each section, and who recites after each section rather than merely highlighting, will comprehend more, retain more, and write better essays as a direct result (Artis, 2018, p. 90).


The four strategies — skimming, scanning, intensive, and extensive reading — and the five-stage SQ3R technique are not abstract academic theories. They are practical tools that produce measurable improvements in academic performance when applied consistently across a semester (Grabe & Stoller, 2020, p. 52).

Which reading strategy is most appropriate when you need to locate a specific date or statistic within a long text?

A.  Skimming
B.  Scanning
C.  Intensive reading
D.  Extensive reading

[2 marks]

3Explain

4Multiple Choice

In the SQ3R technique, what is the purpose of the "Recite" stage?

A.  To skim the headings before reading begins
B.  To convert headings into questions
C.  To consolidate memory by expressing content in one's own words without looking at the text
D.  To re-read sections that were unclear after a first reading

5Distinguish

A.  Question
B.  Survey
C.  Recite
D.  Review

7Apply

8Write Out

Explain what is meant by critical reading. How does it differ from reading for comprehension? Give two questions a critical reader would ask of an academic text.
