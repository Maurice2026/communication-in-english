Section I

The Verb as the Engine of the Sentence

If the noun is the primary naming unit of English, the verb is its primary action unit — and no element of the sentence carries more grammatical information. The verb encodes tense (when an event occurs), aspect (how an event unfolds over time), and voice (the relationship between the subject and the action) (Biber et al., 2021, p. 220). Mastery of these three dimensions is fundamental to accurate and professional academic writing (Swales & Feak, 2020, p. 62).

Three Dimensions of the Verb
Tense
Locates an event in time — past, present, or future. English has two morphological tenses (past and present/non-past); future time is expressed through modal auxiliaries and other constructions (Huddleston & Pullum, 2019, p. 160).
Aspect
Describes the internal temporal structure of an event — whether it is complete, ongoing, or has current relevance. English has two aspects: perfect and progressive (Quirk et al., 2018, p. 125).
Voice
Indicates whether the grammatical subject performs the action (active) or receives it (passive) (Biber et al., 2021, p. 248).
Section II

The Tense-Aspect System

English verbs combine tense and aspect to produce twelve distinct verb forms. Understanding this system is essential for conveying precise temporal meaning in academic writing, where the choice between tenses is rarely arbitrary (Swales & Feak, 2020, p. 65).

Tense & AspectFormAcademic UseExample
Present SimpleV / V-sGeneral truths, habitual actions, reporting what an author claims"Smith (2021) argues that…"
Past SimpleV-edCompleted events at a specific past time; methodology sections"The researchers collected data in 2022."
Future Simplewill + VPredictions, announcements, promises"This paper will examine three factors."
Present Progressiveam/is/are + V-ingOngoing actions; current trends in the field"Researchers are increasingly focusing on…"
Past Progressivewas/were + V-ingOngoing past action interrupted by another event"The experiment was running when the power failed."
Present Perfecthave/has + V-enPast events with current relevance; review of literature"Studies have shown a consistent pattern."
Past Perfecthad + V-enEvents completed before another past event"The data had been collected before the methodology was revised."
Present Perfect Progressivehave/has been + V-ingOngoing action that began in the past and continues now"Scholars have been debating this issue since 2015."
Past Perfect Progressivehad been + V-ingOngoing past action completed before another past event"They had been collecting samples for three months before the analysis began."
Future Progressivewill be + V-ingActions that will be in progress at a future point"The committee will be reviewing applications in March."
Future Perfectwill have + V-enActions that will be completed by a future time"By the end of the study, the team will have interviewed 200 participants."
Future Perfect Progressivewill have been + V-ingDuration of ongoing action up to a future point"By December, they will have been conducting research for two years."
📌

Critical Academic Tense: The present perfect is the most frequently misused tense by Nigerian students. It signals that a past event has current relevance — it is the standard tense for literature reviews: "Research has demonstrated..." not "Research demonstrated..." unless a specific past date is given (Swales & Feak, 2020, p. 70).

Section III

Active and Passive Voice

Voice determines the relationship between the grammatical subject and the action expressed by the verb. English has two voices: active and passive. The choice between them is not merely stylistic — in academic writing, it is a rhetorical decision that determines emphasis, formality, and objectivity (Biber et al., 2021, p. 250).

Active Voice

In the active voice, the grammatical subject is the agent — the one who performs the action. Active sentences are typically more direct, vigorous, and concise (Crystal, 2019, p. 128).

Passive Voice

In the passive voice, the grammatical subject is the recipient of the action. The agent (doer) may be expressed in a by-phrase or omitted entirely. The passive is formed with be + past participle (Quirk et al., 2018, p. 136).

Active Voice
Passive Voice
"The researcher conducted three experiments."
"Three experiments were conducted by the researcher."
"The committee approved the proposal."
"The proposal was approved by the committee."
"Smith (2021) argues that the data is inconclusive."
"It has been argued that the data is inconclusive."
"The students will submit their dissertations in May."
"The dissertations will be submitted in May."
When to Use Each Voice in Academic Writing
Use Active Voice when:
The agent is important and should be emphasised · The sentence needs to be direct and concise · Attributing a claim to a specific scholar: "Chomsky (2018) argues that…" (Swales & Feak, 2020, p. 75).
Use Passive Voice when:
The action or result is more important than who performed it · The agent is unknown, unimportant, or obvious · Achieving objectivity and formality in scientific reporting: "The samples were analysed using gas chromatography." · Avoiding first-person constructions where not permitted (Biber et al., 2021, p. 255).
Section IV

Practical Activity: Tense and Voice Identification

The ability to identify verb tense and voice in academic texts — and to convert between them — is a core skill for both reading comprehension and writing production (Gass & Selinker, 2020, p. 112).

✏️
Practical Activity
Identify Tense and Voice — 10 Sentences

For each sentence, identify (a) the tense-aspect combination and (b) the voice (active or passive). For items 6–10, convert the sentence to the opposite voice without changing the tense.

  • 1
    "The committee reviews all applications annually."
    Identify the tense and aspect — then state the voice.
    Pres. Simple · Active
  • 2
    "The data had been collected before the analysis began."
    Two verb phrases — focus on the main one.
    Past Perf. · Passive
  • 3
    "Researchers have demonstrated a clear correlation."
    Which tense signals "past event with current relevance"?
    Pres. Perfect · Active
  • 4
    "The paper was peer-reviewed by three experts."
    Identify the tense and note the by-phrase.
    Past Simple · Passive
  • 5
    "Scholars have been debating this hypothesis since 2018."
    Ongoing past action continuing to now — which aspect?
    Pres. Perf. Prog. · Active
  • 6
    "The university approved the new curriculum."
    Convert to passive — retain past simple tense.
    Convert → Passive
  • 7
    "The results were published in a reputable journal."
    Convert to active — you may supply a logical agent.
    Convert → Active
  • 8
    "The government will fund the research project."
    Convert to passive — retain future simple tense.
    Convert → Passive
  • 9
    "The proposals have been evaluated by a panel of professors."
    Convert to active — use the agent in the by-phrase as the subject.
    Convert → Active
  • 10
    "Researchers are currently conducting three major studies."
    Convert to passive — retain present progressive tense.
    Convert → Passive
Section V

Conclusion & Module 2 Summary

The verb is the most grammatically complex element of the English sentence. Its tense locates events in time, its aspect characterises their internal structure, and its voice controls the focus of information within the clause. A student who commands these three dimensions can write with precision, objectivity, and stylistic flexibility — the hallmarks of mature academic prose (Biber et al., 2021, p. 260).


The passive voice, in particular, deserves special attention in academic writing — it is not a grammatical weakness but a purposeful rhetorical tool, deployed strategically to achieve the impersonal, evidence-centred tone that characterises formal scholarly writing (Swales & Feak, 2020, p. 78).
