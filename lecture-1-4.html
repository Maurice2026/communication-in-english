Section I

The Power of Phonological Autonomy

The three preceding lectures equipped the student with the building blocks of English phonology: the 44 phonemes, the articulatory mechanisms of vowels and consonants, and the suprasegmental features of stress and intonation. This final lecture of Module 1 converts that knowledge into a practical skill: the ability to independently decode the pronunciation of any unfamiliar English word using a standard dictionary (Roach, 2022, p. 56).

The tool that makes this independence possible is the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA). Developed by the International Phonetic Association in 1888 and subsequently refined, the IPA provides a unique symbol for every distinct sound in every human language (Ladefoged & Johnson, 2020, p. 36). Once a student can read IPA, they no longer need to hear a word pronounced to know how it sounds — the dictionary entry becomes a complete pronunciation guide.

Key Concept
The International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)
A standardised system of phonetic notation using symbols to represent the sounds of all human languages. In English dictionaries, IPA transcriptions are enclosed in forward slashes: /word/. The system allows the reader to determine pronunciation independently of spelling (Davenport & Hannahs, 2020, p. 7).
Phonetic Transcription
The act of converting a spoken or written word into its IPA symbol sequence. This is the central practical skill of applied phonetics (Skandera & Burleigh, 2019, p. 42).
Section II

Reading a Dictionary Entry

Most academic dictionaries — Oxford, Cambridge, Longman, Merriam-Webster — follow a standard format. Understanding each component of a dictionary entry is essential for academic language development (Cruttenden, 2021, p. 52):

education noun BrE/ˌedʒuˈkeɪʃn/    AmE/ˌedʒuˈkeɪʃn/
The process of receiving or giving systematic instruction, especially at a school or university.
① Forward slashes / / — enclose the phonetic transcription; symbols inside represent sounds, not letters.
② Primary stress mark ˈ — placed before the stressed syllable: /ˌedʒuˈkeɪʃn/ — primary stress falls on "KAY".
③ Secondary stress mark ˌ — placed before a syllable with lighter secondary stress: the first syllable carries a secondary stress.
④ Schwa ə — the unstressed syllable "-tion" is reduced to /ʃn/ in rapid connected speech.

The structure is consistent across entries: headword → part of speech → phonetic transcription → definition. A student who can decode this structure can extract full pronunciation information from any dictionary entry they encounter (Roach, 2022, p. 60).

📌

Key Convention: The stress mark ˈ is placed before the syllable it stresses — not after. This is a common source of error. In /ˌedʒuˈkeɪʃn/, the primary stress falls on the syllable that begins with /k/, not the one before the mark (Cruttenden, 2021, p. 54).

Section III

British English vs. American English Transcriptions

Most modern academic dictionaries provide two phonetic transcriptions — one for British English (BrE) and one for American English (AmE). For Nigerian students, BrE has traditionally served as the standard for academic writing and formal speech (Gut, 2019, p. 99). However, global exposure to digital media, social platforms, and North American academic publications means familiarity with AmE is increasingly important (Deterding, 2018, p. 321).

Section IV

Addressing Local Interference — A Practical Guide

Throughout this module, the concept of first-language interference has been introduced. This section addresses specific, documented phonological substitutions common among Nigerian English speakers and shows how dictionary IPA transcriptions provide the precise target for correction (Oyebola & Gut, 2020, p. 175; Aremo, 2021, p. 518).

Intelligibility vs. Identity
The Goal is Intelligibility — Not Accent Erasure
As Jenkins (2022, p. 18) and Deterding (2018, p. 329) both argue, the target for learners of English as an additional language is not native-speaker phonology, but international intelligibility — the ability to be clearly understood across linguistic and cultural contexts. Nigerian English is a legitimate and recognised variety of World English (Gut, 2019, p. 112). The corrections above target the specific features most likely to impede comprehension, not those that simply mark Nigerian identity.

Section VI

Conclusion & Module 1 Summary

With this lecture, the student has acquired the final tool needed for phonological self-sufficiency: the ability to decode any dictionary entry and use IPA to verify pronunciation independently. Combined with the knowledge of the 44 phonemes, the organs of speech, and the suprasegmental features of stress and intonation, the student is now phonologically equipped for the academic work ahead (Roach, 2022, p. 70).


As Deterding (2018, p. 330) argues, the goal of this entire module has been international intelligibility — ensuring that the student's spoken English is clear enough to be understood in any formal context, regardless of their regional background. The IPA is the map. The student now knows how to read it.
