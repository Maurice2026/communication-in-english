Section I

Clauses and Phrases — The Units of Sentence Structure

Lecture 2.1 established that every word in English belongs to a class. This lecture examines how those words combine into the primary unit of communication: the sentence. Understanding sentence structure is indispensable for academic writing, where the ability to vary sentence type signals intellectual sophistication and rhetorical control (Swales & Feak, 2020, p. 32).

Before examining sentence types, two foundational terms must be defined precisely — the phrase and the clause — since sentences are built from these units (Huddleston & Pullum, 2019, p. 86).

Foundational Distinctions
Phrase
A group of words built around a head word, functioning as a single grammatical unit. A phrase does not contain both a subject and a finite verb. Examples: the comprehensive report (noun phrase); very carefully (adverb phrase); in the library (prepositional phrase) (Biber et al., 2021, p. 95).
Clause
A grammatical unit that contains at minimum a subject and a finite verb. A clause may be independent (able to stand alone as a sentence) or dependent (requiring attachment to an independent clause) (Quirk et al., 2018, p. 85).
Independent Clause
A clause that expresses a complete thought and can stand alone as a sentence: "The student submitted the assignment."
Dependent (Subordinate) Clause
A clause that cannot stand alone — it depends on an independent clause to complete its meaning: "…although the deadline had passed." (Yule, 2020, p. 88).
Section II

Structural Sentence Types

English sentences are classified structurally according to the number and type of clauses they contain. There are four structural types, arranged here in ascending complexity — a progression that mirrors the expected development of academic writing style from introductory to advanced level (Swales & Feak, 2020, p. 38).

Type 1
Simple Sentence
Contains exactly one independent clause and no dependent clauses. Despite its name, a simple sentence can have compound subjects or compound predicates and still remain structurally simple (Huddleston & Pullum, 2019, p. 90).
"The researcher collected data."  ·  "Students and lecturers debated the findings and proposed solutions."
Formula: [Independent Clause]
Type 2
Compound Sentence
Contains two or more independent clauses joined by a co-ordinating conjunction (FANBOYS: for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so) or a semicolon. Each clause could stand alone as a sentence (Biber et al., 2021, p. 102).
"The data was collected, but the analysis revealed unexpected results."  ·  "He wrote the report; she edited it."
Formula: [Independent Clause] + co-ordinator + [Independent Clause]
Type 3
Complex Sentence
Contains one independent clause and one or more dependent clauses introduced by subordinating conjunctions (although, because, when, if, since, unless, whereas) or relative pronouns (who, which, that) (Quirk et al., 2018, p. 94).
"Although the sample size was limited, the findings were statistically significant."  ·  "The student who submitted first received the highest mark."
Formula: [Independent Clause] + [Dependent Clause] (in any order)
Type 4 — Advanced
Compound-Complex Sentence
Contains two or more independent clauses AND one or more dependent clauses. This is the hallmark of advanced academic prose — it allows the writer to express multiple relationships simultaneously (Swales & Feak, 2020, p. 45).
"Although the methodology was contested, the data supported the hypothesis, and subsequent studies confirmed the initial findings."
Formula: [Independent] + [Dependent] + co-ordinator + [Independent]
📌

Academic Writing Tip: Over-reliance on simple sentences creates choppy, underdeveloped prose. Over-reliance on compound-complex sentences can obscure meaning. The mark of a skilled academic writer is the conscious, purposeful variety of sentence structure (Swales & Feak, 2020, p. 47).

Section III

Functional Sentence Types

Sentences are also classified by their communicative function — what the speaker or writer intends to achieve with the utterance (Crystal, 2019, p. 109). These functional types cut across the structural types: any structural type can perform any function.

TypeFunctionTypical FormExample
Declarative States a fact, opinion, or piece of information — the default sentence type in academic writing Subject + Verb (+ Object/Complement) "The experiment confirmed the hypothesis."
Interrogative Asks a question, inviting a response Auxiliary + Subject + Verb (Yes/No) or Wh-word + Auxiliary + Subject + Verb "Did the results support the theory?"  /  "What caused the anomaly?"
Imperative Issues a command, instruction, or request; the subject you is typically omitted Base form of verb (+ Object) "Submit your work before Friday."  /  "Consider the following evidence."
Exclamatory Expresses strong emotion or emphasis; rare in formal academic writing What/How + noun phrase / adjective phrase "What a remarkable discovery this proved to be!"

In academic and professional writing, declaratives dominate, supported selectively by imperatives in instructions and methodology sections. Interrogatives appear in rhetorical questions, and exclamatives are almost entirely absent from formal registers (Biber et al., 2021, p. 118).

Section IV

Practical Activity: Identify and Combine

Accurate identification of sentence type is the prerequisite for deliberate manipulation of sentence structure in academic writing (Thornbury, 2019, p. 88). The activity below tests both identification and construction skills.

✏️
Practical Activity
Identify the Sentence Type — 10 Sentences

For each sentence below, identify both its structural type (Simple / Compound / Complex / Compound-Complex) and its functional type (Declarative / Interrogative / Imperative / Exclamatory). Write one sentence justifying your structural classification.

  • 1
    "The committee reviewed the proposals."
    One subject, one verb, no dependent clause — identify both types.
    Simple · Declarative
  • 2
    "The report was thorough, but the recommendations were vague."
    Two independent clauses joined by "but" — identify both types.
    Compound · Declarative
  • 3
    "Although funding was limited, the project succeeded."
    One dependent clause + one independent clause — identify both types.
    Complex · Declarative
  • 4
    "Submit your assignment by noon."
    No stated subject — the implied subject is "you" — identify both types.
    Simple · Imperative
  • 5
    "Have you read the supplementary text?"
    Auxiliary precedes subject — identify both types.
    Simple · Interrogative
  • 6
    "Because the data was incomplete, the researchers extended the study, and a revised paper was submitted."
    Count the clauses — how many independent? How many dependent?
    Compound-Complex · Declarative
  • 7
    "What a significant contribution this research has made to the field!"
    Strong emotion expressed through What + noun phrase structure.
    Simple · Exclamatory
  • 8
    "The lecturer explained the concept, and the students took detailed notes."
    Two independent clauses, each with subject and verb — joined by "and".
    Compound · Declarative
  • 9
    "The student who scored highest in the examination was awarded a scholarship."
    Relative clause "who scored highest" — dependent on the main clause.
    Complex · Declarative
  • 10
    "Read the passage carefully and answer all ten questions."
    Two imperative clauses joined by "and" — identify both structural and functional types.
    Compound · Imperative
Section V

Conclusion & Key Takeaway

The four structural sentence types — simple, compound, complex, and compound-complex — are not merely grammatical categories. They are rhetorical tools. The deliberate selection of sentence type shapes how ideas are presented, how relationships between ideas are signalled, and how a reader experiences the logic of an argument (Swales & Feak, 2020, p. 50).


A student who can identify clauses, distinguish independent from dependent, and consciously vary sentence structure will produce academic prose that is not only grammatically correct but stylistically compelling. This is the goal of the entire Grammar and Usage module (Biber et al., 2021, p. 120).

1Multiple Choice

Which of the following is the correct definition of a dependent clause?

A.  A clause that contains a subject and a finite verb and can stand alone as a sentence
B.  A group of words without a finite verb functioning as a single unit
C.  A clause that cannot stand alone and requires attachment to an independent clause
D.  A clause joined to another clause using a co-ordinating conjunction

[1 mark]

2Identify

Identify the structural type of each sentence and justify your answer in one sentence:  (a) "She studied all night."   (b) "She studied all night, but she still failed the test."   (c) "Although she studied all night, she still failed."

[3 marks — 1 per sentence]

3Write Out

Distinguish between a phrase and a clause. Write one original example of each and explain why your example fits the definition.

[2 marks]

4Multiple Choice

A compound-complex sentence contains:

A.  One independent clause and no dependent clauses
B.  Two or more independent clauses and no dependent clauses
C.  One independent clause and one or more dependent clauses
D.  Two or more independent clauses and one or more dependent clauses

[1 mark]

5Combine

Combine the following three simple sentences into one compound-complex sentence without losing any information:  "The lecturer arrived late. The students had already begun. They were discussing the previous lecture."

[2 marks]

6Multiple Choice

In the sentence "Submit your work before the deadline," the functional type is:

A.  Declarative
B.  Interrogative
C.  Imperative
D.  Exclamatory

[1 mark]

7Identify & Label

In the sentence "Although the methodology was questioned, the findings were accepted, and the paper was published in a peer-reviewed journal," identify: (a) the number of independent clauses, (b) the number of dependent clauses, and (c) the structural sentence type.

[3 marks]

8Write Out

List the seven FANBOYS co-ordinating conjunctions. For each, write an original sentence demonstrating its correct use in joining two independent clauses.

[3 marks]

9Multiple Choice

Which of the following is a complex sentence?

A.  "The dean spoke and the students listened."
B.  "The results were published."
C.  "Because funding was secured, the project continued."
D.  "Read the instructions and answer the questions."

[1 mark]

10Short Essay

In a paragraph of 6–8 sentences, explain why sentence variety is described as a marker of academic writing sophistication. Use specific examples of each structural type — simple, compound, complex, and compound-complex — to support your argument.
