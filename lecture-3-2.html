Section I

Hearing vs. Listening — A Critical Distinction

Of the four primary language skills — reading, writing, speaking, and listening — listening is the most frequently used and the least formally taught (Vandergrift & Goh, 2019, p. 1). Research indicates that tertiary-level students spend approximately 50–75% of their classroom time listening, yet most receive almost no explicit instruction in how to do it effectively (Gilakjani & Sabouri, 2021, p. 124).

The first and most important distinction to establish is that hearing and listening are not the same thing. One is physiological; the other is cognitive and purposeful (Rost, 2020, p. 7).

Hearing vs. Listening
Hearing
A passive, involuntary physiological process — the reception of sound waves by the ear. Hearing requires no effort or intent and occurs continuously while a person is awake (Rost, 2020, p. 8).
Listening
An active, deliberate, cognitive process — the conscious selection, attention, comprehension, and interpretation of what is heard. Listening requires mental effort, purpose, and engagement (Vandergrift & Goh, 2019, p. 4).
Active Listening
The highest form of listening — involving not only comprehension but also critical evaluation, emotional intelligence, and responsive feedback. Active listening is a learnable skill that can be deliberately practised and improved (Brownell, 2018, p. 22).
Section II

Types of Listening

Not all listening situations are identical. Different academic and professional contexts call for different types of listening, each with a distinct purpose and requiring different cognitive processes (Rost, 2020, p. 14).

TypePurposeAcademic Context
Discriminative Distinguishing between sounds, words, and phonemes — the most basic level of listening, essential for understanding spoken language at all (Vandergrift & Goh, 2019, p. 16). Following a lecture in a second language · Understanding an unfamiliar accent
Comprehensive Understanding the full message — its content, organisation, and meaning (Brownell, 2018, p. 28). Listening to a lecture · Following instructions · Attending a seminar presentation
Critical Evaluating the content for logic, credibility, evidence, and bias. The listener questions and analyses rather than merely absorbing (Rost, 2020, p. 19). Panel discussions · Oral examinations · Listening to political or academic debates
Empathic Listening to understand the speaker's feelings, perspective, and emotional state — suspending judgement to fully enter the speaker's frame of reference (Brownell, 2018, p. 34). Counselling contexts · Group discussions where personal experiences are shared · Peer support
Appreciative Listening for enjoyment, aesthetic pleasure, or entertainment. The listener is not focused on information or evaluation (Gilakjani & Sabouri, 2021, p. 127). Listening to poetry readings · Attending a lecture by an inspiring scholar · Oral storytelling
📌

Academic Priority: In tertiary-level academic contexts, comprehensive and critical listening are the most important types. Comprehensive listening ensures you understand what is being communicated; critical listening ensures you can evaluate, question, and build upon it (Rost, 2020, p. 22).

Section III

Barriers to Effective Listening

Even motivated listeners regularly fail to listen effectively because of barriers — internal and external factors that interfere with the reception, processing, or retention of spoken information (Brownell, 2018, p. 44). Identifying these barriers is the first step to overcoming them.

Internal Barrier
Pseudo-listening
Appearing to listen — maintaining eye contact and nodding — while mentally absent. One of the most common barriers in lecture settings (Vandergrift & Goh, 2019, p. 28).
Internal Barrier
Selective Listening
Hearing only what you want or expect to hear, filtering out information that challenges existing beliefs or seems unimportant (Brownell, 2018, p. 47).
Internal Barrier
Cognitive Overload
The speaker delivers information faster than the listener can process it, causing comprehension breakdown. Common in technical or highly specialised lectures (Rost, 2020, p. 35).
Internal Barrier
Emotional Interference
Strong emotional reactions to the speaker or content — anxiety, anger, boredom, or prejudice — that prevent objective processing of the message (Gilakjani & Sabouri, 2021, p. 130).
External Barrier
Environmental Noise
Physical distractions in the listening environment — background conversation, traffic noise, poor acoustics, or interruptions (Brownell, 2018, p. 50).
External Barrier
Speaker Characteristics
Unclear pronunciation, an unfamiliar accent, speaking too fast or too slowly, or monotonous delivery that reduces listener engagement (Vandergrift & Goh, 2019, p. 31).
Section IV

Note-Taking as a Listening Strategy

The most powerful strategy for improving listening in academic settings is structured note-taking. Note-taking forces active engagement with the spoken content — the listener must process, select, and record information simultaneously, preventing passive or pseudo-listening (Dunkel & Pialorsi, 2018, p. 12).

There are several established note-taking methods, each suited to different types of academic listening situations (Rost, 2020, p. 48):

Structured Note-Taking Methods
Cornell Method
Divide the page into three sections: a narrow left column for key words and questions, a wide right column for notes, and a summary section at the bottom. Ideal for lectures (Dunkel & Pialorsi, 2018, p. 14).
Outlining
Record main ideas as headings, with supporting details and examples indented beneath. Most effective when the speaker has a clear, hierarchical structure (Rost, 2020, p. 50).
Mind Mapping
Place the main topic in the centre and branch outward to subtopics and details. Effective for lectures that cover a broad topic with many interrelated concepts (Brownell, 2018, p. 58).
Charting
Create columns for different categories of information. Particularly effective when a lecture compares or contrasts multiple items across consistent categories (Dunkel & Pialorsi, 2018, p. 18).
Sentence Method
Write each new piece of information as a complete sentence on a new line. Simple and comprehensive but can be slow — best used when listening pace allows time for full sentences (Vandergrift & Goh, 2019, p. 38).
📌

Key Principle: Note-taking is not transcription. A student who tries to write every word the lecturer says will always fall behind and miss the next point. The goal is to capture key ideas, evidence, and relationships — not every word (Dunkel & Pialorsi, 2018, p. 20).

Section V

Practical Activity: Listening Awareness

✏️
Practical Activity
Listening Skills — 10 Scenario Questions

For each scenario, identify the type of listening required, the barrier present, or the appropriate note-taking method. Write one sentence justifying your answer.

  • 1
    A student nods and maintains eye contact during a lecture but cannot recall a single point afterwards.
    Identify the specific listening barrier demonstrated.
    Pseudo-listening
  • 2
    During a panel debate, you evaluate each speaker's argument for logical consistency and credibility.
    Which type of listening is this?
    Critical Listening
  • 3
    A professor speaks very rapidly and uses highly technical vocabulary throughout the lecture.
    Identify the external barrier present.
    Speaker Characteristic
  • 4
    Your lecturer's lecture compares three theories across the same five criteria. Which note-taking method is most efficient?
    Think about the structure of the content.
    Charting
  • 5
    A classmate shares a personal experience of academic failure. What listening mode do you adopt?
    The goal is understanding feelings, not evaluating arguments.
    Empathic Listening
  • 6
    You are attending a research presentation. What note-taking method uses a central topic with radiating branches?
    Identify the visual, non-linear method.
    Mind Mapping
  • 7
    A student only pays attention to points that confirm what he already believes and ignores contradictory evidence.
    Name the internal listening barrier.
    Selective Listening
  • 8
    During a lecture, you write a full sentence for each new idea as soon as it is stated. Which method is this?
    Identify the straightforward, comprehensive method.
    Sentence Method
  • 9
    Distinguish in one sentence: why is a student who hears a lecture but cannot summarise it not truly listening?
    Apply the hearing vs. listening distinction.
    Hearing ≠ Listening
  • 10
    You listen to a spoken word poet perform at a university event purely for aesthetic appreciation.
    Which type of listening does not require evaluation or comprehension focus?
    Appreciative Listening
Section VI

Conclusion & Key Takeaway

Listening is not a passive ability that students either have or do not have — it is an active, learnable skill that improves with deliberate practice. A student who can distinguish the types of listening, recognise the barriers that obstruct it, and apply structured note-taking will perform significantly better in lectures, seminars, oral examinations, and group discussions (Vandergrift & Goh, 2019, p. 55).


The most important shift this lecture asks of the student is from passive hearing to active listening — from sitting in a lecture hall and hoping information will lodge in the memory, to deliberately engaging, questioning, and recording what is heard (Rost, 2020, p. 60).
