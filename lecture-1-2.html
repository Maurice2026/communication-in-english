Section I

The Vowel Quadrilateral — Classifying Monophthongs

Lecture 1.1 established that English has 12 monophthong vowels. This lecture explains precisely how those vowels are classified and produced. Vowels in English are not simply "A, E, I, O, U" — they are described systematically using three articulatory criteria: tongue height, tongue backness, and lip position (Roach, 2022, p. 10). The traditional tool for mapping these relationships is the Vowel Quadrilateral, a diagram developed by the International Phonetic Association (Ladefoged & Johnson, 2020, p. 17).

Figure 2: The Vowel Quadrilateral mapping the 12 English monophthongs by tongue position (front–central–back) and tongue height (close–mid–open). Adapted from Ladefoged & Johnson (2020, p. 18) and Cruttenden (2021, p. 30).

The Long vs. Short Vowel Distinction

A critical challenge for Nigerian students is the vowel merger — pronouncing long and short vowels identically, thereby confusing listeners (Oyebola & Gut, 2020, p. 172). The following pairs must be clearly distinguished in both production and perception:

Section II

Consonant Classification: The Three Criteria

Every consonant in English can be fully and precisely described using exactly three labels: its voicing, its place of articulation, and its manner of articulation (Davenport & Hannahs, 2020, p. 22). For example, the sound /b/ is a voiced bilabial plosive; the sound /f/ is a voiceless labio-dental fricative. Mastering these three parameters allows the learner to describe, compare, and correct any consonant sound systematically (Skandera & Burleigh, 2019, p. 38).

Criterion 1: Voicing

As established in Lecture 1.1, consonants are either voiced (vocal cords vibrate) or voiceless (vocal cords remain open). Test by placing two fingers on the larynx: voiced sounds produce a perceptible buzz (Roach, 2022, p. 31).

Voiced / Voiceless Pairs
Voiced examples
/b/ bat  ·  /d/ dog  ·  /g/ get  ·  /v/ van  ·  /z/ zoo  ·  /ð/ this
Voiceless counterparts
/p/ pat  ·  /t/ top  ·  /k/ cat  ·  /f/ fan  ·  /s/ sun  ·  /θ/ thin

Criterion 2: Place of Articulation

Place of articulation identifies where in the vocal tract the obstruction or narrowing occurs (Ladefoged & Johnson, 2020, p. 30):

Places of Articulation
Bilabial
Both lips meet. Produces /p/, /b/, /m/. E.g. pan, ban, man.
Labio-dental
Upper teeth contact lower lip. Produces /f/, /v/. E.g. fan, van.
Dental
Tongue tip touches or approaches upper teeth. Produces /θ/ thin, /ð/ this.
Alveolar
Tongue tip contacts the alveolar ridge (just behind upper teeth). Produces /t/, /d/, /n/, /s/, /z/, /l/.
Palato-alveolar
Tongue blade near the back of the alveolar ridge. Produces /ʃ/ she, /ʒ/ measure, /tʃ/ church, /dʒ/ judge.
Velar
Back of tongue contacts the soft palate (velum). Produces /k/, /g/, /ŋ/ sing.
Glottal
Constriction at the level of the glottis (vocal cords). Produces /h/ hat.

Criterion 3: Manner of Articulation

Manner of articulation describes how the airstream is modified at the place of obstruction (Cruttenden, 2021, p. 38):

Manners of Articulation
Plosive
Airflow is completely blocked then suddenly released as a burst. E.g. /p/, /b/, /t/, /d/, /k/, /g/.
Fricative
Airflow is narrowed to create turbulent friction. E.g. /f/, /v/, /s/, /z/, /θ/, /ð/, /ʃ/, /ʒ/, /h/.
Affricate
Begins as a plosive and releases as a fricative. E.g. /tʃ/ church, /dʒ/ judge.
Nasal
Oral cavity is blocked; air escapes through the nose. E.g. /m/, /n/, /ŋ/.
Lateral
Tongue blocks the centre of the mouth; air escapes around the sides. E.g. /l/.
Approximant
Articulators approach each other but do not create friction. E.g. /r/, /w/, /j/.
Section III

The Schwa /ə/ — The Most Important Sound in English

Among all 44 phonemes, the schwa /ə/ deserves special attention. It is the most frequently occurring vowel sound in spoken English — more common than any other phoneme — and yet it receives almost no attention in formal spelling instruction (Roach, 2022, p. 14). The schwa is a short, mid-central, unstressed vowel that appears in the reduced syllables of virtually every polysyllabic English word (Skandera & Burleigh, 2019, p. 30).

The Schwa /ə/ in Academic Vocabulary
about
/əˈbaʊt/ — the 'a' is reduced to schwa
teacher
/ˈtiːtʃə/ — the 'er' suffix is typically schwa in BrE
director
/dɪˈrektə/ — the final 'or' reduces to schwa
education
/ˌedʒuˈkeɪʃən/ — multiple unstressed syllables carry schwa
government
/ˈɡʌvənmənt/ — both unstressed syllables carry schwa
📌

Academic Tip: Many Nigerian students over-pronounce unstressed syllables — giving each vowel its full written value. Using the schwa correctly on unstressed syllables creates the natural rhythm of English and significantly improves intelligibility (Deterding, 2018, p. 324).

Section IV

Practical Activity: Minimal Pair Drill

A minimal pair is two words that differ by exactly one phoneme. Drilling minimal pairs is one of the most effective methods for training phonemic discrimination — the ability to hear and produce contrasting sounds clearly (Jenkins, 2022, p. 10). The pairs below target the specific contrasts most challenging for Nigerian English speakers (Gut, 2019, p. 108).

Section V

Conclusion & Key Takeaway

Understanding the where and how of sound production is the foundation of phonological self-correction. When a word does not sound right, the speaker can now ask a precise question: Is my tongue in the correct position? Am I voicing or devoicing correctly? Is the vowel long or short? (Cruttenden, 2021, p. 42).


The three criteria for consonant classification — voicing, place of articulation, and manner of articulation — provide a complete and systematic vocabulary for describing any sound in English. These tools will be used throughout the remaining lectures of this module and across the entire course (Roach, 2022, p. 35).
