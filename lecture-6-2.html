Section I

Academic Email Writing

Email is the primary formal channel of communication in Nigerian universities and in professional environments. It is the medium through which students correspond with lecturers, submit formal requests, communicate with the university administration, and increasingly, conduct academic and professional networking. Despite its centrality, email writing is one of the most poorly executed communication skills among Nigerian students (Nwosu & Obi, 2021, p. 68).

An email to a lecturer or a professional contact is a formal document that reflects your intelligence, your care, and your respect for the recipient. A poorly written email — with no salutation, unclear purpose, informal language, or typographical errors — creates a negative impression that can affect how you are perceived academically and professionally. A well-written email demonstrates competence, maturity, and professionalism.

The Seven Components of a Well-Written Academic Email
1. A Clear, Specific Subject Line
The subject line is the first thing the recipient sees. It must tell them immediately what the email is about and whether it requires action. Vague subject lines ("Help needed", "Question") are unhelpful. Clear subject lines ("Request for Extension — ENG 201 Essay, Due 15 May") allow the recipient to prioritise and respond appropriately (Guffey & Loewy, 2021, p. 112).
2. A Formal and Appropriate Salutation
Address the recipient by their correct title and surname: "Dear Dr. Udom," / "Dear Professor Eze," / "Dear Mr. Adeyemi," / "Dear Mrs. Okafor," — always followed by a comma. Never use first names when addressing lecturers or administrators unless explicitly invited to. Never use "Hi" or "Hello" in a formal academic email. If you do not know the title, "Dear Dr./Prof. [Surname]" is safe; never use "Dear Sir/Ma'am" as your opening in email to a named individual.
3. A Contextualising Opening Sentence
Begin by identifying yourself (your name, department, and year of study) and stating the purpose of the email in one clear sentence. Do not assume the recipient knows who you are — they may have hundreds of students. Example: "My name is Adaeze Okonkwo, a 200-level student in the Department of English and Literary Studies. I am writing to request a brief extension on the ENG 201 essay due on 15 May."
4. A Concise and Clearly Organised Body
The body should address one topic per email. Provide all relevant context in the most concise form possible. Use short paragraphs (3–5 sentences each). If multiple points are needed, use numbered or bulleted lists rather than one dense paragraph. The reader should be able to understand your request and what response you need without re-reading.
5. A Polite and Specific Request or Question
State clearly what you need from the recipient. "Could you please advise me on..." / "I would be grateful if you could..." / "Please find attached..." Use conditional and courteous phrasing. Never demand. Never assume. Remember that the recipient is under no obligation to respond immediately, and acknowledge this if appropriate: "I understand you have a busy schedule and would appreciate any guidance at your convenience."
6. A Professional Closing
End with a formal sign-off: "Yours sincerely," (when you know the recipient's name) / "Yours faithfully," (when you do not). Follow with your full name, department, student number or staff designation, and any relevant contact information. "Thanks," "Cheers," or "Best," are too informal for academic emails to lecturers or administrators.
7. Proofreading Before Sending
Always read your email at least once before clicking Send. Check for spelling errors, grammatical mistakes, tone inconsistencies, missing attachments (if promised), and correct recipient address. One careless email can undermine weeks of professional impression-building (Guffey & Loewy, 2021, p. 118).

Email Writing — Annotated Examples

To: dr.udom@uniuyo.edu.ng
Subject: Request for Extension — ENG 301 Essay, Submission Date 20 May 2026
Dear Dr. Udom,

My name is Chukwuemeka Adiele, a 300-level student in the Department of English and Literary Studies (Student No. 2021/ENG/0347). I am writing to respectfully request a short extension on the ENG 301 essay currently due on 20 May 2026.

I have been experiencing a significant health issue since last week, which has substantially reduced my ability to complete the required research and writing. I have attached a medical certificate from the University Health Centre dated 12 May 2026 in support of this request. I am seeking an extension of five working days, which would move my submission date to 27 May 2026.

I understand that this request is at your discretion and I appreciate you considering it. Please let me know if any further documentation is required or if an alternative arrangement would be more appropriate.

Yours sincerely,
Chukwuemeka Adiele
300-Level, English and Literary Studies
Student No. 2021/ENG/0347
Tel: 080X-XXX-XXXX
Why this email works: Specific, informative subject line. Immediate self-identification. Clear purpose stated in the first sentence. Supporting evidence (medical certificate) mentioned and attached. Specific and reasonable request (5 days, not "a few weeks"). Acknowledgement of lecturer's discretion. Professional closing with full identification. The email is courteous, concise, and provides everything the lecturer needs to make a decision.
To: dr.udom@uniuyo.edu.ng
Subject: pls help
Good day sir,

Am emeka i am your student. I need extension because i am sick and cannot finish the work. Please give me more time. I will submit when am well.

Thanks
Emeka
Why this email fails: Subject line is uninformative and unprofessional. "Am" instead of "I am" throughout (grammatical errors). No student number, department, or course code. No specific extension period requested. No supporting documentation mentioned. No formal salutation or sign-off. The email creates an impression of carelessness and disrespect, and does not give the lecturer enough information to act on the request — even if they are sympathetic. This is unfortunately a very common type of email received by Nigerian university lecturers.
Section II

Netiquette — The Rules of Digital Conduct

Netiquette (a portmanteau of "network" and "etiquette") refers to the accepted standards of polite, respectful, and appropriate behaviour in digital communication environments. Just as face-to-face interaction is governed by social norms, digital interaction has its own evolving conventions — and violating them can have real consequences for your academic and professional reputation (Shea, 2019, p. 22).

✓ Digital Communication Do's
  • Match your tone to the platform and audience — formal for email, more casual for class group chats, always professional in academic forums
  • Respond to emails and messages within a reasonable time (24–48 hours for academic emails)
  • Use a clear, professional email address — your university-issued address or a variation of your real name (e.g., c.adiele@gmail.com, not "slimboy99@yahoo.com")
  • Acknowledge receipt of information even if you cannot respond fully immediately
  • Credit others for their ideas when sharing content digitally — attribution is as important online as in written essays
  • Re-read messages before sending to ensure they convey the intended tone
  • Use appropriate subject lines and keep messages focused and concise
  • Respect others' time by not sending unnecessary "reply-all" messages
✗ Digital Communication Don'ts
  • Never type in ALL CAPS in digital communication — it is the digital equivalent of shouting and is considered aggressive and rude
  • Never share personal information about others without their consent — including photographs taken at private events
  • Do not forward emails or messages beyond their intended audience without permission
  • Never use WhatsApp voice notes for formal academic communication with lecturers or administrators
  • Do not use excessive emoji or informal abbreviations (LOL, BRB, LMAO) in academic or professional digital communication
  • Never post or comment in anger — take a pause before responding to upsetting digital content
  • Do not participate in online harassment, pile-ons, or cyberbullying in any form, including sharing posts that target individuals
  • Never share copyrighted material (articles, textbook chapters, films) through academic group chats without proper licensing
📌

The Permanence Principle: Before posting, sending, or sharing anything digitally, apply this test: Would I be comfortable if this message were seen by my future employer, my lecturer, my parents, and a newspaper journalist simultaneously? If no, do not send it. Digital content is rarely truly temporary — screenshots, archives, and cached versions can preserve what you thought was deleted. Your digital communication is part of your permanent record (Shea, 2019, p. 30).

Section III

Online Academic Discourse

Academic discourse is the specialised mode of communication used in scholarly communities — characterised by precision, evidence-based argument, acknowledgement of sources, appropriate hedging language, and intellectual rigour. As academic activity moves increasingly into digital spaces (online lectures, virtual seminars, discussion forums, academic social networks), students must learn to maintain the standards of academic discourse in these new environments (Herring, 2020, p. 44).

Online Discussion Forums and Virtual Seminars

Many universities now use learning management systems (LMS) such as Moodle, Blackboard, or Google Classroom, which include discussion forum features. Contributions to these forums are academic contributions — they should demonstrate the same quality of reasoning and expression as a seminar contribution or a short essay response.

Standards for Online Academic Discussion Posts
Substantive Content
A discussion post should contribute something meaningful to the academic conversation — an argued position, a relevant example, a critical question, or a synthesis of ideas. Responses like "I agree with Adaeze" or "Great point!" are not academic contributions. They add nothing to the intellectual discourse (Herring, 2020, p. 46).
Evidence and Citation
Even in informal online academic discussions, claims should be supported by reference to course materials, readings, or credible sources. You need not use formal APA citation in every discussion post, but you should identify your sources: "In the reading by Hurley and Watson (2018), the argument is that..." This practice builds the habit of evidence-based reasoning.
Respectful Disagreement
Online academic discourse requires the ability to disagree with a classmate's or lecturer's position without being dismissive, aggressive, or personal. Academic disagreement is about ideas, not people. Phrases such as "I take a different view from [Name]'s position, because the evidence suggests..." demonstrate intellectual maturity and appropriate discourse conduct.
Appropriate Register
Online academic forums require a register that is more formal than WhatsApp but can be slightly less formal than a written essay. Avoid slang, emoji in serious posts, and the abbreviations of instant messaging. Write in complete sentences. Proof-read before submitting. Your posts in an academic forum are visible to your lecturer and all classmates — they form part of your academic record and participation grade.
Section IV

Digital Identity and Professional Reputation

Your digital identity is the sum of all information about you that exists in digital form — your social media profiles, your posts and comments, photographs, videos, forum contributions, professional profiles, and even information others have posted about you. In an age when employers, universities, and professional bodies routinely conduct digital searches of candidates, your digital identity is as important as your physical one (Hobbs, 2020, p. 68).

Personal Digital Identity

How you present yourself in personal digital spaces — Instagram, TikTok, personal Facebook, WhatsApp status. These are primarily social but are not private from the professional world unless carefully managed.

Key rule: Apply the Permanence Principle to everything. Content shared in "personal" spaces regularly crosses into professional visibility, particularly content involving professional opinions, political statements, or behaviour that reflects on character.

Academic Digital Identity

How you present yourself in academic digital spaces — university email, LMS discussion forums, ResearchGate, Google Scholar profiles, academic Twitter/X. These spaces directly reflect your academic competence and scholarly identity.

Key rule: Maintain consistent professional standards. Even casual academic online spaces reflect your intellectual character. Build a positive academic digital footprint by making thoughtful contributions to online scholarly discussions.

Professional Digital Identity

How you present yourself in professional digital spaces — LinkedIn, professional email, company or institutional social media, professional networking platforms. These are seen directly by employers, clients, and professional bodies.

Key rule: Invest in your professional digital profile from your university years. A well-maintained LinkedIn profile with academic achievements, relevant projects, and professional endorsements gives you a significant advantage in the Nigerian and global job market.

Building a Positive Professional Digital Presence

Rather than merely avoiding digital mistakes, digitally literate graduates actively build positive professional digital identities. This involves creating a LinkedIn profile while still at university, contributing to academic online discussions, maintaining a professional email address, and using digital platforms to showcase skills, projects, and achievements. In Nigeria’s competitive job market, digital visibility that demonstrates academic and professional competence is a real advantage (Ojo & Dike, 2022, p. 55).

Section V

Research Ethics in the Digital Age

The digital age has dramatically expanded access to information and research tools, but it has also created new ethical challenges for academic researchers at all levels — from undergraduate students to senior academics. Understanding the ethics of digital research is essential for maintaining academic integrity and avoiding serious consequences (Bawden, 2021, p. 142).

Ethical IssueWhat It InvolvesThe Academic Standard
Copyright and Fair Use Most content online — articles, images, videos, software — is protected by copyright even when freely accessible. Downloading, sharing, or reproducing copyrighted content without permission may be illegal, and using it in academic work without attribution is plagiarism. Use content that is licensed for reuse (Creative Commons), properly attributed, or obtained through authorised academic databases. Never reproduce more than a short quotation from any copyrighted source, and always cite. Never photocopy or digitally share full textbooks or journal articles in class groups without authorisation.
Plagiarism and AI Misuse Copying from online sources without citation is plagiarism. Submitting AI-generated text (from ChatGPT, Gemini, or similar tools) as your own original work is a form of academic fraud increasingly addressed by university regulations worldwide — including in Nigerian universities. All submitted work must be your own original thinking and writing. Sources must be cited. AI tools may be used to support research (checking grammar, exploring ideas) only where explicitly permitted by the lecturer or institution. When in doubt, ask. Turnitin and AI-detection tools are now routinely used by universities.
Online Research Participant Privacy Students conducting research involving data collected from social media, online surveys, or digital platforms must handle participant data responsibly — ensuring anonymity, obtaining informed consent where required, and not using data in ways participants would not expect or consent to. Follow your institution’s research ethics guidelines. In general: do not collect personal data without consent, anonymise data wherever possible, and do not store personal data insecurely. Refer to Nigeria’s Data Protection Regulation (NDPR 2019) for the legal framework.
Source Attribution Online The informal culture of the internet encourages sharing without attribution — memes, quotations, and images circulate without credit to their creators. Academic work requires the opposite: every source must be identified, credited, and cited in the appropriate format. Apply the same citation standards to digital sources as to print sources. Websites, online articles, YouTube videos, podcasts, and tweets used in academic work must be cited in APA, MLA, or the format required by your institution. Use Zotero or Mendeley to manage digital citations systematically.
Section VI

Social Media and Academic Communication

Social media platforms — particularly X (Twitter), LinkedIn, ResearchGate, and academic Facebook groups — have become important venues for scholarly communication, professional networking, and knowledge dissemination. Nigerian academics and students who understand how to navigate academic social media have access to global scholarly conversations, emerging research, and professional opportunities that were unavailable to previous generations (Nwosu & Obi, 2021, p. 88).

Academic and Professional Social Media Use
X (Formerly Twitter) — Academic Twitter (#AcademicTwitter)
X has a significant academic community where researchers share their findings, discuss emerging ideas, post about conferences and calls for papers, and engage in scholarly debate. Nigerian academics and students can follow global thought leaders, access pre-publication research threads, and build networks beyond their institution. The key is to engage substantively — sharing ideas, asking genuine questions, and contributing to discussions — rather than merely scrolling (Herring, 2020, p. 62).
LinkedIn — Professional Academic Networking
LinkedIn is the world’s largest professional network and increasingly important for academic career development. A strong LinkedIn profile includes: a professional photograph, a summary of your academic background and research interests, your educational qualifications, relevant skills and certifications, and connections with classmates, lecturers, and professionals in your field. Begin building this profile in your first year of university — not in your final year when you are suddenly looking for employment.
ResearchGate and Google Scholar Profiles
As you begin producing academic work — even undergraduate essays that develop into publishable research — maintaining a ResearchGate profile and a Google Scholar profile allows you to share your academic work, track citations, and be discoverable by the global scholarly community. These platforms are the academic equivalents of a professional portfolio.
What to Avoid on Academic Social Media
Avoid posting uninformed opinions on technical academic debates you are not qualified to adjudicate. Avoid aggressive or dismissive engagement with other academics' work. Avoid sharing unverified claims in academic spaces — your professional credibility depends on the accuracy of what you post. Avoid oversharing personal information. And always maintain awareness that what you post under your real name in academic contexts is permanent and publicly searchable (Ojo & Dike, 2022, p. 60).

Practical Activity — Section VIII
Digital Communication in Practice — 10 Exercises

Apply the digital communication skills from both lectures of Module 6 to the following exercises. All written responses should be produced at the appropriate level of formality for their context.

  • 1Write a formal email to a fictitious lecturer (Dr. Amina Bello, Department of Communication Arts) requesting clarification on an essay question for a course she teaches. Apply all seven components of a well-written academic email. Your email must be 150–200 words.
  • 2A classmate sends you the following email asking for feedback: "hi can u help me with my assignment its due tomorrow and i dont know wut to do pls help im in ur class". Rewrite this email so it would be appropriate to send to a lecturer. Identify at least five specific problems with the original.
  • 3Review the list of Netiquette Do's and Don'ts. Identify THREE of the "Don'ts" that you have personally observed being violated in an academic digital context (such as a class WhatsApp group). For each, describe the situation and explain what harm the violation caused or could cause.
  • 4A lecturer posts the following question in your course online discussion forum: "Based on this week's readings, how do you think social media affects the quality of public discourse in Nigeria?" Write a discussion post of 120–150 words that meets the academic discourse standards outlined in this lecture.
  • 5Search your own name on Google. What do you find? What image does your current digital footprint project? What changes, if any, would you make to improve your academic and professional digital identity? Write a reflection of 150–200 words.
  • 6Identify a case of each of the following from real Nigerian digital experience: (a) copyright infringement online, (b) AI or plagiarism misuse in academic contexts, (c) privacy violation involving personal data. For each, describe the situation and identify the specific ethical violation.
  • 7Create or review your LinkedIn profile. Ensure it includes: a professional photograph, an accurate academic summary, your current institution and year of study, three relevant skills, and at least two connections. Share your profile URL with your class group and review two classmates' profiles, offering one constructive observation for each.
  • 8A fellow student tells you: "It's fine to use ChatGPT to write my essay — I just rephrase it a bit and it's original enough." Using the concepts from this lecture and from Module 5 (critical thinking), construct a well-reasoned response that addresses the ethical, academic, and practical problems with this position.
  • 9You discover that a close friend has posted a screenshot of a private WhatsApp conversation from your class group on their public Facebook page, including comments made by named classmates. Apply the concepts of malinformation, netiquette, digital safety, and the Permanence Principle to analyse what has happened and what the appropriate response might be.
  • 10Write a 200-word personal Digital Literacy Action Plan. Identify: (a) your current strongest pillar, (b) your weakest pillar, (c) one specific new tool or habit you will adopt from Module 6, and (d) one aspect of your digital communication behaviour you will change based on what you have learned. This plan should be specific and actionable, not vague.
Conclusion

Course Completion — Final Reflection

Module 6 completes the Communication in English course. You have travelled from the foundational sounds of English (Module 1: Phonetics and Phonology) through the rules of grammar (Module 2), the four core language skills (Module 3), academic writing across multiple genres (Module 4), the principles of logical and critical reasoning (Module 5), and finally to the digital literacy competencies that govern communication in the twenty-first century (Module 6) (Bawden, 2021, p. 200).


The unifying thread across all six modules is this: effective communication is always conscious, purposeful, and audience-aware. Whether you are pronouncing a word, constructing a sentence, presenting a speech, writing an essay, evaluating an argument, or composing a professional email — the questions are always the same: What am I trying to say? Who am I saying it to? What is the appropriate form, register, and medium? And how do I ensure my communication is accurate, honest, and ethical? These are the questions of a Communication in English graduate. Carry them with you into every professional and personal context your future holds (Nwosu & Obi, 2021, p. 110).
