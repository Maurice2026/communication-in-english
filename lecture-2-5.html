Section I

What Is a Sentence?

The sentence is the fundamental unit of communication in English. All written and spoken language — from a casual text message to a doctoral thesis — is ultimately constructed from sentences. Yet defining the sentence precisely is more complex than it first appears (Huddleston & Pullum, 2019, p. 85).

A traditional definition states that a sentence is "a group of words expressing a complete thought." While useful as a starting point, this definition is insufficient for grammatical analysis because it relies on the intuitive notion of "completeness" rather than on structural criteria (Quirk et al., 2018, p. 44). A more rigorous definition requires that a sentence must contain at minimum:

Definition and Minimum Requirements
The Sentence — Formal Definition
A grammatically complete unit of language that contains at minimum a subject and a finite verb, expresses a proposition, and is capable of standing alone as an utterance (Biber et al., 2021, p. 89).
Subject
The noun phrase about which the predicate makes a statement. Every sentence must have a subject — though in imperative sentences, the subject you is typically implied rather than stated (Crystal, 2019, p. 106).
Finite Verb
A verb that is marked for tense and agrees with its subject in person and number. A group of words lacking a finite verb — however long — is a phrase or clause, not a sentence (Huddleston & Pullum, 2019, p. 87).
📌

Key Test: Ask two questions — (1) Is there a finite verb? (2) Does the verb have a subject? If both answers are yes, the unit is a sentence. "Running through the hall" fails Test 1 — no finite verb. "Which the committee approved" fails Test 2 — no independent subject-verb pairing (Quirk et al., 2018, p. 46).

Section II

Types of Sentences by Structure

Sentences are classified structurally according to the number and type of clauses they contain. This classification determines the syntactic complexity of the sentence and has direct implications for academic writing style (Swales & Feak, 2020, p. 38).

Figure 4: The four structural types of English sentences, showing their clause composition. Adapted from Huddleston & Pullum (2019, p. 92) and Quirk et al. (2018, p. 48).

Type 1
Simple Sentence
One independent clause only. May have a compound subject or compound predicate but remains structurally simple (Biber et al., 2021, p. 96).
"The professor submitted the report."  ·  "Ahmed and Fatima passed the examination."
Formula: [Independent Clause]
Type 2
Compound Sentence
Two or more independent clauses joined by a co-ordinating conjunction (FANBOYS) or semicolon. Each clause could stand alone (Quirk et al., 2018, p. 52).
"The data was collected, but the analysis was incomplete."  ·  "He wrote the draft; she revised it."
Formula: [IC] + co-ordinator + [IC]
Type 3
Complex Sentence
One independent clause and one or more dependent (subordinate) clauses, introduced by subordinating conjunctions or relative pronouns (Crystal, 2019, p. 111).
"Although the result was unexpected, the methodology was sound."  ·  "The student who scored highest received a scholarship."
Formula: [IC] + [DC] (in any order)
Type 4 — Advanced
Compound-Complex Sentence
Two or more independent clauses AND one or more dependent clauses. The hallmark of advanced academic prose, allowing simultaneous expression of multiple logical relationships (Swales & Feak, 2020, p. 45).
"Although the sample was small, the findings were significant, and the paper was accepted for publication."
Formula: [IC] + [DC] + co-ordinator + [IC]
Section III

Types of Sentences by Function

Every sentence is also produced for a communicative purpose — a function. English recognises four functional sentence types. These cut across structural types: a simple sentence can be declarative, interrogative, imperative, or exclamatory (Crystal, 2019, p. 114).

TypeFunctionForm MarkerExample
Declarative States a fact, opinion, or piece of information. The dominant type in academic writing (Biber et al., 2021, p. 118). Subject precedes finite verb "The study confirmed the hypothesis."
Interrogative Asks a question, inviting a response or information (Quirk et al., 2018, p. 58). Auxiliary precedes subject (Yes/No); Wh-word at front (Wh-question) "Did the results support the theory?" / "What caused the anomaly?"
Imperative Issues a command, instruction, or request. Subject you is omitted (Huddleston & Pullum, 2019, p. 95). Base form of verb; no stated subject "Read the passage carefully." / "Consider the following evidence."
Exclamatory Expresses strong emotion or surprise. Rare in formal academic writing (Crystal, 2019, p. 115). Introduced by What or How "What a remarkable contribution this study has made!"
Section IV

Parts of the Sentence — Subject and Predicate

Every sentence divides into two major constituents: the subject and the predicate. This binary division is the most fundamental grammatical analysis a student can perform on any sentence (Quirk et al., 2018, p. 66).

Subject and Predicate — Definitions
Subject
The noun phrase that the sentence is about. It is the topic of the predicate's statement. In declarative sentences, the subject typically appears before the finite verb. The subject controls verb agreement — it determines whether the verb is singular or plural (Huddleston & Pullum, 2019, p. 100).
Predicate
Everything in the sentence that is not the subject — the finite verb and all that accompanies it. The predicate makes a statement, asks a question, or gives a command about the subject (Biber et al., 2021, p. 125).
"The senior lecturer published a comprehensive textbook last year."
Subject The senior lecturer
Noun phrase — controls verb agreement
Predicate published a comprehensive textbook last year
Everything said about the subject

Within the predicate, it is possible to identify further components — the verb, the object, and the complement. Each plays a distinct grammatical role (Crystal, 2019, p. 107).

Section V

The Verb

The verb is the core of the predicate — the element without which no predicate can exist. Every complete sentence contains at least one finite verb (Biber et al., 2021, p. 127). For the purposes of sentence analysis, verbs are classified by the type of structure they require:

Verb TypeDefinitionRequiresExample
Transitive Carries action from the subject to an object — the action passes across to something (Quirk et al., 2018, p. 74). A direct object to complete meaning "She wrote a letter." — wrote is transitive; a letter is the object
Intransitive Does not carry action to an object — the action remains with the subject (Huddleston & Pullum, 2019, p. 108). No object; sentence is complete without one "The child slept." / "The crowd cheered loudly."
Linking (Copular) Connects the subject to a subject complement — describes or identifies the subject rather than expressing an action (Biber et al., 2021, p. 130). A subject complement (not an object) "The result seems conclusive." / "She became the director."

Common linking verbs include: be, become, seem, appear, feel, look, taste, smell, sound, grow, remain, stay, turn. They are crucially different from transitive verbs because what follows them is a complement, not an object (Crystal, 2019, p. 109).

Section VI

The Object — Direct and Indirect

When a transitive verb is used, the sentence requires an object — the noun phrase that receives or is affected by the action of the verb. English distinguishes between two types of object (Quirk et al., 2018, p. 80):

Direct Object and Indirect Object
Direct Object (DO)
The noun phrase that directly receives the action of the verb. Test: ask What? or Whom? after the verb — the answer is the direct object (Huddleston & Pullum, 2019, p. 112). Example: "She sent a letter." — What did she send? A letter.
Indirect Object (IO)
The noun phrase that indicates the recipient or beneficiary of the action — the entity for whom or to whom the direct object is given, sent, or shown (Biber et al., 2021, p. 133). The indirect object always co-occurs with a direct object and typically precedes it. Test: ask To whom? or For whom? Example: "She sent the dean a letter." — To whom did she send it? The dean.
"The registrar sent the students their examination timetables."
Subject The registrar
Agent performing the action
Verb sent
Transitive — requires both objects
Indirect Object the students
Recipient — to whom it was sent
Direct Object their examination timetables
What was sent — receives the action
📌

Order Rule: When both a direct and indirect object are present without a preposition, the indirect object comes first: "Give me the report." When the preposition to or for is used, the direct object comes first: "Give the report to me." Both are grammatically correct (Quirk et al., 2018, p. 82).

Section VII

The Complement — Subject and Object Complement

A complement is a word or phrase that completes the meaning of the verb by describing or identifying either the subject or the object. Unlike an object, a complement does not receive an action — it provides information about an entity already mentioned in the sentence (Biber et al., 2021, p. 138). There are two types:

Subject Complement (SC)

A subject complement follows a linking verb and describes or identifies the subject. It may be a noun phrase, adjective phrase, or prepositional phrase (Huddleston & Pullum, 2019, p. 116).

"The new professor became the head of department."
Subject The new professor
Who the sentence is about
Linking Verb became
Connects subject to complement
Subject Complement the head of department
Identifies the subject — answers "What did she become?"

Further examples of subject complements: "The result was conclusive." (adjective phrase) · "She remained the director for three years." (noun phrase) · "The food tastes bitter." (adjective) (Crystal, 2019, p. 110).

Object Complement (OC)

An object complement follows the direct object and describes or identifies it. It is related to the direct object in the same way a subject complement is related to the subject (Quirk et al., 2018, p. 88).

"The senate appointed Professor Nwosu the Vice-Chancellor."
Subject The senate
Agent performing the action
Verb appointed
Transitive verb
Direct Object Professor Nwosu
Who received the appointment
Object Complement the Vice-Chancellor
Identifies what the DO became

Further examples: "They found the report incomplete." (adjective OC) · "The committee elected her chairperson." (noun phrase OC) · "His speech left the audience speechless." (adjective OC) (Biber et al., 2021, p. 140).

Distinguishing Object from Complement
The Renaming Test
A complement renames or describes another element. A direct object receives an action. Ask: is this element saying something about the subject or object? If yes, it is a complement. If it independently receives an action, it is an object (Huddleston & Pullum, 2019, p. 118).
The Linking Verb Clue
Subject complements always follow linking verbs. If you can replace the verb with a form of be and the sentence still makes sense, the complement is a subject complement: "She became the director" → "She was the director." ✓

Section IX

Conclusion & Key Takeaway

The sentence is not merely a string of words — it is a precisely organised grammatical structure, every element of which carries a specific syntactic function. The ability to distinguish subject from predicate, transitive from linking verb, direct from indirect object, and subject complement from object complement is the foundation of grammatical self-correction in academic writing (Quirk et al., 2018, p. 92).


A student who has internalised the parsing framework presented in this lecture can approach any sentence — in their own writing or in a scholarly text — and account for every element within it. This analytical rigour is what separates grammatically confident academic writers from those who write by intuition alone (Swales & Feak, 2020, p. 52).
