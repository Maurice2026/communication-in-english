Section I

What Is a Letter? Types and Purposes

A letter is a written message addressed from one person or organisation to another, typically covering a specific subject and composed in a form appropriate to the relationship between writer and recipient. Despite the rise of email and digital communication, formal letter writing remains a crucial skill in Nigerian academic, professional, legal, and governmental contexts (Guffey & Loewy, 2021, p. 148).

Letters are classified in two major ways: by formality level (formal or informal) and by purpose. Understanding the purpose of a letter helps determine its appropriate structure, tone, and vocabulary.

Section II

Components of a Formal Letter

A formal letter in the Nigerian and international convention contains specific components that must appear in the correct position and format. Each component has a precise function (Nwofor, 2019, p. 22).

The Eight Components of a Formal Letter
1. Writer’s Address (Top Right)
Your full postal address, written in the top right-hand corner. Include house or flat number, street name, town/city, and state. Do not include your name here. Example: 15 Azikiwe Road, Uyo, Akwa Ibom State. In formal Nigerian convention, the date is written directly below the address.
2. Date
Write the full date in unambiguous format, directly below the writer’s address. Write out the month in full to avoid confusion: 15 May 2026 (not 15/5/26, which is ambiguous internationally). Do not abbreviate: "May" not "May."
3. Recipient’s Address (Left-Aligned)
The full name, title, and address of the person or organisation receiving the letter, written on the left-hand side below the date. Include: title and name (if known), position/designation, organisation name, full postal address. If the recipient’s name is not known, use their title only: The Director of Admissions.
4. Salutation
The formal opening greeting, aligned left: Dear Dr. Udom, / Dear Sir, / Dear Madam, / Dear Sir/Madam, (when gender is unknown). Always followed by a comma in Nigerian convention. Never use first names in a formal letter salutation unless specifically invited to do so.
5. Subject Line (Reference Line)
A brief, capitalised line directly below the salutation identifying the specific subject of the letter. This allows the recipient to understand the letter’s purpose at a glance and facilitates filing. Format: RE: APPLICATION FOR THE POSITION OF RESEARCH ASSISTANT. The "RE:" prefix is conventional in Nigerian formal letters.
6. Body
The main content of the letter, divided into paragraphs. The first paragraph states the purpose of the letter. Middle paragraphs develop the content with relevant details, evidence, or argument. The final paragraph states what action is requested or expected and thanks the recipient. Each paragraph should have a single clear focus and be separated by a blank line.
7. Complimentary Close
The formal sign-off before the signature. Use: Yours sincerely, when you know the recipient’s name (used with "Dear [Name]"). Use: Yours faithfully, when you do not know the recipient’s name (used with "Dear Sir/Madam"). These are not interchangeable — the rule is absolute (Guffey & Loewy, 2021, p. 155).
8. Signature
Your handwritten signature, followed by your printed full name below it, and (if relevant) your designation or title. Example: [Signature] / Chukwuemeka Adiele / 300-Level Student, Department of English and Literary Studies.
📌

The Sincerely/Faithfully Rule: "Yours sincerely" is used when the salutation contains a name (Dear Dr. Udom). "Yours faithfully" is used when the salutation does not contain a name (Dear Sir/Madam). This rule is fixed in formal English letter-writing convention and is tested in examinations (Nwofor, 2019, p. 30).

Section III

Model Formal Letter — Fully Annotated

Study the following model formal letter carefully. Each component is correctly positioned and formatted according to standard Nigerian formal letter-writing convention.

Model Formal Letter — Complaint Letter
Room 22, Block C, Student Hostel,
University of Uyo,
Uyo, Akwa Ibom State.
15 May 2026
The Director,
Student Affairs Division,
University of Uyo,
Uyo, Akwa Ibom State.
Dear Sir/Madam,
RE: DETERIORATING WATER SUPPLY CONDITIONS IN BLOCK C STUDENT HOSTEL
I write on behalf of the students residing in Block C of the university hostel to formally bring to your attention the severely deteriorating water supply conditions we have been experiencing since the commencement of the second semester in February 2026.
For the past three months, residents of Block C have had access to running water on fewer than four days per week, with complete absence of water supply on weekends being a regular occurrence. Despite multiple verbal complaints to the hostel warden, no remedial action has been taken. The situation has caused significant hardship and poses a serious public health risk, particularly during the current period of elevated temperatures.
We respectfully request that your office investigate this matter urgently and take immediate steps to restore consistent water supply to Block C. We further request that a formal acknowledgement of this complaint be issued within five working days, and that a timeline for restoration of services be communicated to the affected residents.
We trust that this matter will receive the urgent attention it deserves. We are available to provide further information or meet with your representative at your convenience.
Yours faithfully,

[Signature]
Ngozi Okonkwo
Block C Hostel Representative
Student Number: 2022/ENG/0178
Analysis: Note the correct use of "Yours faithfully" because the salutation is "Dear Sir/Madam" (name unknown). The subject line is in full capitals and begins with "RE:". The letter is organised into four clear paragraphs: purpose, problem details, specific requests, closing. The tone is firm and assertive without being aggressive. Every component is in the correct position.
Section IV

Informal Letters — Structure and Style

Informal letters are personal communications addressed to family members, friends, or close acquaintances. Their structure is less rigid than formal letters, and their tone may be warm, conversational, humorous, or intimate — depending on the relationship between writer and recipient (Harmer, 2020, p. 112).

Components of an Informal Letter
Address (Top Right)
Your address, written in the top right corner. The format is the same as a formal letter, but you may omit the recipient’s address entirely in an informal letter since it is a personal communication, not a document requiring filing.
Date
Written below the address. The format may be slightly less formal: "15 May 2026" or "15th May 2026" are both acceptable.
Salutation
Warm and personal: Dear Kemi, / Dear Uncle Tunde, / My dear Adaeze, / Dear Mum,. First names or familiar titles are appropriate. The salutation is followed by a comma.
Body
Conversational in style. May begin with an enquiry about the recipient’s wellbeing: "I hope this letter meets you in good health and high spirits." Content is personal, anecdotal, and may range freely across topics. Paragraphs are less strictly structured than in formal letters.
Complimentary Close
Warm and personal: Yours lovingly, / Yours sincerely, / With love, / Best wishes, / Take care,. The appropriate close depends on the intimacy of the relationship.
Signature
First name only is standard in informal letters to people who know you well: Emeka / Your daughter, Adaeze.

Formal vs. Informal Letters — Comparison

FeatureFormal LetterInformal Letter
Recipient’s addressRequired — full name, title, address on leftOptional — usually omitted
SalutationDear Dr. Obi / Dear Sir/MadamDear Kemi / My dear Uncle / Dear Mum
Subject lineRequired — in capitals, prefixed "RE:"Not required
ToneFormal, precise, objective, impersonalWarm, conversational, personal, relaxed
LanguageStandard formal English; no contractions; no slangContractions acceptable; colloquial language appropriate
First person"I write to inform you..." (formal first person)"I’m so excited to tell you..." (natural first person)
Complimentary closeYours sincerely / Yours faithfully (rule-governed)With love / Best wishes / Yours (flexible)
SignatureFull name + title/designationFirst name only usually sufficient
📌

Common Error — Register Confusion: The most common letter-writing error is using informal language in a formal letter or overly stiff language in an informal one. In a formal letter, avoid contractions ("I’m" → "I am"), slang, Nigerian Pidgin, excessive familiarity, and emotional language. In an informal letter, avoid the coldness of formal register unless the letter is actually dealing with a serious matter (Paltridge, 2018, p. 78).

Section VI

Practical Writing Activity

🖌
Practical Activity — Section VI
Letter Writing Exercises — 10 Tasks

Complete all letter writing tasks below. Each letter must use the correct format, appropriate register, and include all required components.

  • 1Write a formal letter to the Vice Chancellor of your university requesting the establishment of a student mental health support centre on campus. Your letter must include all eight components of a formal letter and a compelling, well-organised body.
  • 2Write an informal letter to a close friend who has just been admitted to the university of their first choice. Congratulate them warmly, share a relevant personal anecdote, and offer practical advice for their first year.
  • 3Write a formal complaint letter to the Director of Academic Affairs about consistent delays in the release of examination results in your department. Be specific about the problem, its impact, and what remedy you are requesting.
  • 4Identify and correct ALL errors in the following letter opening: "Dear Sir Udom, I am a student of English dept. Am writing this letter to ask you about my score. I think i was marked unfair and i want to complain about this. Yours sincerely, Emeka"
  • 5Write a formal letter from a student organisation requesting permission from the Registrar to use the university auditorium for a public lecture. Include the date, expected attendance, purpose, and assurances regarding venue care.
  • 6Explain why "Yours sincerely" and "Yours faithfully" are not interchangeable. Write two example letter salutations and endings showing the correct use of each, with an explanation of the rule that governs the choice.
  • 7Write an informal letter to your parent or guardian explaining why you need extra financial support for a particular semester. Your letter must be honest, appropriately detailed, and demonstrate respect for the recipient without being manipulative.
  • 8Convert the following informal letter opening into a formal register: "Hey Registrar! How are you doing? I’m Tunde and am in 300 level. I wanna ask about my transcript cos I need it for grad school application. When can I come get it? Let me know ASAP!"
  • 9Write a formal letter to a company whose product you have used, commending the quality of their service. A positive letter of commendation is as professionally important as a complaint — it builds goodwill and demonstrates communication competence.
  • 10Compare and contrast a formal and an informal letter by writing both versions on the same topic: asking for a postponement of a planned meeting. Version A: a formal request to a university panel. Version B: an informal request to a friend. Annotate each letter to highlight the key differences in format, language, and tone.
Conclusion

Key Takeaway

Letter writing — both formal and informal — is a practical communication skill with immediate real-world application. In Nigeria, formal letters are used to petition government agencies, apply for positions, lodge complaints with institutions, and communicate officially in virtually every sector of professional life. Mastery of formal letter conventions — the correct positioning of components, the appropriate register, the Sincerely/Faithfully rule, and the professional tone — marks you as a competent communicator (Nwofor, 2019, p. 48).


Lecture 4.4 completes Module 4 by examining two of the most consequential genres of professional writing you will encounter as a graduating student: the curriculum vitae (CV) and the letter of application — the two documents that will open the door to your career (Guffey & Loewy, 2021, p. 180).
