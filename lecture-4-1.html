Section I

What Is an Essay?

An essay is a structured piece of writing designed to present an idea, argument, or narrative in a coherent and logical manner. In academic contexts, essays are not merely expressive compositions but analytical tools used to demonstrate understanding and critical thinking (Bailey, 2018).

Contemporary research emphasises that essay writing is central to academic success because it integrates reading, thinking, and writing into a single intellectual activity (Wingate, 2020). Therefore, an essay must go beyond storytelling to include organisation, argumentation, and clarity.

At the tertiary level, the essay is the primary instrument by which students demonstrate their grasp of course content, their ability to reason critically, and their command of academic language. Every examination question and assignment brief ultimately demands this same skill. Understanding what an essay is — and what it is not — is the first step toward writing one well.

Key Concept
Academic Essay — Definition
A structured piece of writing that presents a central idea, argument, or analysis in a logical, coherent, and purposeful manner. Unlike casual writing, the academic essay must demonstrate understanding, integrate evidence, and communicate ideas with precision and clarity (Bailey, 2018).
Essay as an Analytical Tool
In academic contexts, the essay goes beyond personal expression. It functions as an instrument of intellectual inquiry — one that integrates reading comprehension, critical reasoning, and formal written expression into a unified performance. Essay writing is therefore central to academic literacy (Wingate, 2020).
📌

Foundational Principle: An essay is not a collection of loosely related ideas — it is a unified argument that moves logically from a clear beginning, through a developed middle, to a purposeful end. Every sentence must earn its place by contributing to that movement (Lunsford, 2019).

Section II

The Structure of an Essay

A standard academic essay consists of three major parts. This structure is not arbitrary — it mirrors the movement of a well-formed argument: from statement, through support, to resolution. Every essay, regardless of length or subject, is built on this same framework (Oshima & Hogue, 2020).

Figure 1: The standard three-part essay structure. All academic essays — regardless of length or subject — are built on this framework (Oshima & Hogue, 2020).

1
Introduction
10–15%

The introduction provides background information and presents the thesis statement, which expresses the central idea of the essay. Its purpose is to orient the reader — to establish what the essay is about and why it matters.

A strong introduction must accomplish three things:

  • Engage the reader with a compelling opening — a striking fact, a thought-provoking question, or a relevant anecdote
  • Provide sufficient context or background information so the reader understands the topic
  • End with a clear and specific thesis statement that announces the essay's central argument

The introduction should not contain arguments, detailed evidence, or the conclusion. Its role is to set the stage, not to perform (Lunsford, 2019).

2
Body Paragraphs
70–80%

The body develops the argument or main idea through a series of paragraphs. Each body paragraph must be unified — addressing one central idea — and must contribute directly to proving the thesis.

Each paragraph must contain the following four elements (Hyland, 2019):

  • A topic sentence — states the paragraph's one central idea; the paragraph's mini-thesis
  • Supporting details or evidence — facts, examples, quotations, statistics that back the topic sentence
  • Explanation or analysis — the writer's interpretation of how the evidence supports the point
  • A linking sentence — connects smoothly to the next paragraph or back to the thesis

Effective paragraphing improves readability and logical flow (Hyland, 2019).

3
Conclusion
10–15%

The conclusion summarises the essay and reinforces the thesis without introducing new ideas. Its function is to bring the essay to a purposeful and memorable close — not to begin a new line of argument.

A strong conclusion must accomplish the following:

  • Restate the main argument (thesis) in fresh language — not word-for-word repetition
  • Briefly summarise the key supporting points made in the body paragraphs
  • Leave a final impression — a broader implication, a call to action, or a closing reflection
  • Never introduce new arguments, evidence, or ideas not already established in the body
Section III

The Thesis Statement

The thesis is the most important part of an essay. It defines the direction of the entire work. Placed at the end of the introduction, it is the single sentence that tells the reader exactly what the essay will prove or demonstrate (Andrews & Smith, 2019).

Without a clear thesis, every paragraph that follows has no clear purpose to serve. The thesis is the anchor of the essay — every body paragraph must connect back to it, and the conclusion must restate it. A strong thesis statement must be:

Qualities of a Good Thesis Statement
Clear
The thesis must be unambiguous. The reader should immediately understand what claim is being made. Vague or obscure language in a thesis produces a vague and directionless essay.
Specific
The thesis must focus on a defined, manageable aspect of the topic — not the entire subject. A specific thesis allows the writer to go into genuine depth rather than covering everything superficially.
Focused
A good thesis makes one clear, central claim. It does not attempt to prove two or three separate things at once. The body of the essay will develop the thesis — the thesis itself should be concise (Andrews & Smith, 2019).

Thesis Statement — Weak vs. Strong

The difference between a weak and strong thesis is the difference between a statement of fact and an arguable, specific claim. Study the following comparison carefully:

✗ Weak Thesis

"Education is important."

This is a statement of obvious fact. No reasonable person would dispute it. It provides no direction, makes no argument, and gives the reader no sense of what the essay will prove.

✓ Strong Thesis

"Education plays a crucial role in national development by improving literacy, promoting innovation, and reducing poverty."

Specific, arguable, and purposeful. It previews three supporting points — one for each body paragraph — and makes a claim that the essay can actually prove (Andrews & Smith, 2019).

📌

The Thesis Test: Before finalising a thesis statement, apply these three questions: (1) Is this an arguable claim — could a reasonable person disagree? (2) Is it specific enough to be proved within the essay? (3) Does it tell the reader exactly what the body paragraphs will demonstrate? If the answer to all three is yes, the thesis is strong. Clarity in thesis writing significantly enhances essay quality (Andrews & Smith, 2019).

Section IV

Paragraph Unity and Coherence

A paragraph must function as a unified unit of meaning. Two essential qualities define a well-constructed academic paragraph: unity and coherence. These two qualities work together — a paragraph may be unified in its ideas but incoherent in its expression, and vice versa. Both must be achieved simultaneously (Oshima & Hogue, 2020).

Anatomy of a Well-Constructed Body Paragraph
Topic Sentence
Definition: The first sentence of the paragraph, which states the one central idea that the paragraph will develop. Every subsequent sentence must relate to this sentence — if a sentence does not support the topic sentence, it does not belong in the paragraph.
Example: "Access to quality primary education is the most critical factor in reducing long-term poverty in developing nations."
Supporting Details
Definition: Facts, examples, statistics, quotations, or expert opinion that provide concrete evidence for the topic sentence. Must be specific and directly relevant — vague generalities weaken a paragraph.
Example: "UNESCO (2022) reports that each additional year of schooling increases an individual's lifetime earnings by approximately 10%."
Analysis / Explanation
Definition: The writer's own explanation of how the evidence supports the topic sentence. This is where critical thinking is demonstrated — evidence does not interpret itself. The writer must always explain the significance of the evidence.
Example: "This statistic demonstrates that education is not merely a social good but a measurable economic investment with generational consequences."
Linking Sentence
Definition: The final sentence, which connects this paragraph to the next — or loops the argument back to the thesis — maintaining the essay's logical flow and forward momentum.
Example: "If primary education yields such significant returns, the case for universal access to schooling becomes both morally and economically compelling."

Unity and Coherence Defined

Two Essential Paragraph Qualities
Unity
A paragraph has unity when every sentence it contains relates to and supports the one central idea stated in the topic sentence. Any sentence that introduces a different idea — even a related one — breaks unity and belongs in a separate paragraph. Poor unity fragments the essay's argument and confuses the reader.
Coherence
A paragraph has coherence when ideas flow logically and smoothly from one sentence to the next, so the reader can follow the development of the argument without confusion. Coherence is achieved through logical sequencing, the use of transition words, and the repetition of key terms. Poor coherence is one of the major causes of weak academic writing (Oshima & Hogue, 2020).

Transition Words for Coherence

Transition words and phrases signal the logical relationship between sentences and paragraphs. Using them correctly significantly improves the coherence and readability of academic writing (Graff & Birkenstein, 2021).

Adding Information
Furthermore, Moreover, In addition, Additionally, Besides, Also
Contrasting / Conceding
However, Nevertheless, On the other hand, Although, Despite this, While
Cause and Effect
Therefore, Consequently, As a result, Thus, Hence, For this reason
Illustrating / Exemplifying
For example, For instance, To illustrate, Specifically, As demonstrated by
Sequencing
First, Second, Third, Subsequently, Then, Finally, Thereafter
Concluding / Summarising
In conclusion, In summary, To summarise, Ultimately, It follows that
Section V

Types of Essays — Introductory Level

At the introductory level, students focus on two essay types that develop the foundational skills of expression and organisation before progressing to the more complex demands of argumentative writing. These two types serve as the training ground for all academic essay writing (Cottrell, 2019).

📖 Narrative Essay

Definition: A narrative essay tells a story — typically a personal experience — in a structured and purposeful way. Unlike casual storytelling, the academic narrative essay uses the story to illustrate or reflect upon a broader theme or insight.

Key Features:

Organised chronologically (events presented in time order) · First-person perspective · Vivid, descriptive language · A clear beginning, rising action, climax, and resolution · A reflective conclusion that draws out the significance of the experience rather than simply ending the story.

Purpose: To develop the student's ability to organise a sequence of events logically while developing a personal voice and descriptive skill in writing.

Example Topic: "My First Day at University" — narrate the experience, then reflect on what it taught you about academic life.

🖼️ Descriptive Essay

Definition: A descriptive essay paints a detailed and vivid picture of a person, place, object, or experience using precise and evocative language. The goal is to create a specific impression in the reader's mind through carefully chosen detail (Bailey, 2018).

Key Features:

Organised spatially, thematically, or chronologically · Rich sensory detail (sight, sound, smell, touch, taste) · Precise vocabulary — concrete nouns and specific adjectives · A dominant impression that unifies the entire description · Figurative language (simile, metaphor) where appropriate.

Purpose: To sharpen the student's observational skill and develop the ability to convey specific, concrete details rather than vague generalities.

Example Topic: "A Place That Influenced My Life" — describe the place through specific sensory details, not abstract statements.

📌

Why Begin with These Types: These two essay types help students develop expression and organisation skills before moving to argumentative writing. Narrative teaches structure and voice; descriptive teaches precision and detail. Both are foundations upon which the argumentative essay — covered in Lecture 4.2 — is built.

Section VI

Practical Writing Task

Writing competence develops only through sustained practice. Apply every concept covered in this lecture to the task below (Thornbury, 2019).

✏️
Practical Activity — Section VI
Descriptive Essay Writing Task
Writing Task
Write a Descriptive Essay (400–600 Words)
Topic: "A Place That Influenced My Life"

Your essay must demonstrate all of the structural and compositional skills taught in this lecture. Focus on creating a clear, organised, and vivid piece of writing that shows — rather than tells — what makes this place significant to you.
  • Write a clear introduction that engages the reader and ends with a thesis statement identifying the place and its significance to you
  • Develop at least three organised body paragraphs, each with a topic sentence, specific sensory details as evidence, analysis of the significance of those details, and a linking sentence
  • Write a strong conclusion that restates your thesis and leaves the reader with a final reflection on how the place shaped you
  • Use at least four different transition words to ensure coherence
  • Avoid vague statements — replace every general claim with a specific, concrete detail
Conclusion

Key Takeaway

The academic essay is the primary vehicle through which tertiary-level intellectual competence is demonstrated and assessed. Mastery of its foundational elements — the three-part structure, the thesis statement, and the well-constructed paragraph — provides the platform upon which all advanced academic writing is built (Wingate, 2020).


A student who can write a clear and specific thesis, develop it through unified and coherent paragraphs, and bring it to a purposeful conclusion has acquired the most transferable writing skill available at the undergraduate level. Every essay type — narrative, descriptive, argumentative, analytical — is a variation on this same structural logic. These foundations are not merely introductory — they are permanent (Graff & Birkenstein, 2021).
