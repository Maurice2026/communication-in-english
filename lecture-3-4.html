Section I

Integration: The Four Skills Working Together

The previous three lectures examined the four language skills individually: reading strategies, listening techniques, and speaking delivery. In practice, however, academic communication rarely isolates one skill at a time. Real academic contexts require the simultaneous and integrated deployment of all four skills (Oxford, 2018, p. 3).

Consider a university seminar: a student must read the assigned text beforehand, listen critically to the lecturer and peers, speak to contribute, argue, and respond, and often write notes, summaries, or a follow-up essay. Each skill reinforces and depends upon the others (Nation & Newton, 2019, p. 12).

Key Concept
Integrated Language Use
The simultaneous deployment of two or more language skills in a single communicative event. Integration is the norm in authentic academic and professional communication — not the exception (Oxford, 2018, p. 5).
Academic Communication
Communication that occurs in a formal educational or scholarly context, governed by conventions of evidence, argumentation, precision, and appropriate register (Hyland, 2019, p. 7).
Section II

Academic Register — Formal vs. Informal

Register is the variety of language appropriate to a particular social situation, purpose, and relationship between speakers or writers (Hyland, 2019, p. 14). One of the most critical transitions a student makes upon entering tertiary education is the shift from informal, conversational register to formal academic register — across all four language skills (Swales & Feak, 2020, p. 12).

FeatureInformal RegisterFormal Academic Register
Vocabulary Colloquial, everyday words: "a lot of," "stuff," "kind of" Precise, academic vocabulary: "considerable," "material," "somewhat" (Hyland, 2019, p. 16)
Contractions Contractions used freely: "it's," "don't," "we've" Contractions avoided: "it is," "do not," "we have"
Person First person freely used: "I think," "I believe" Impersonal or hedged constructions preferred: "It may be argued that," "The evidence suggests" (Swales & Feak, 2020, p. 18)
Sentence Structure Short, fragmented, or loosely connected sentences Complex, well-organised sentences with explicit logical connectors
Evidence Personal opinion stated as fact: "Everyone knows that…" Claims supported by evidence and citation: "Smith (2021) demonstrates that…" (Hyland, 2019, p. 20)
Hedging Absolute statements: "This proves that…" Qualified claims: "This suggests that…," "The data indicates…" (Nation & Newton, 2019, p. 28)
📌

Register Awareness: Register errors in academic writing and oral presentations are noticed by assessors and examiners. Using informal language in a formal academic context signals a failure to understand the conventions of the academic community — regardless of how intelligent the underlying argument is (Hyland, 2019, p. 22).

Section III

Seminar and Discussion Participation

The university seminar is the setting in which integrated language use is most visibly and assessably demonstrated. A student who participates effectively in seminars reads assigned texts beforehand, listens to contributions critically, speaks clearly and relevantly, and frames contributions within the appropriate academic register (Bailey, 2018, p. 78).

Principles of Effective Seminar Participation
Preparation
Read assigned texts before the seminar using the SQ3R technique (Lecture 3.1). Arrive with at least two questions or observations prepared in advance (Cottrell, 2019, p. 115).
Relevance
Contributions must be relevant to the topic under discussion. Before speaking, ask: Does this point advance the argument? Irrelevant contributions waste time and signal poor listening (Nation & Newton, 2019, p. 34).
Respectful Disagreement
Academic discourse depends on the ability to challenge ideas without attacking persons. Use impersonal constructions to separate the argument from the individual: "That position may not account for…" rather than "You are wrong about…" (Hyland, 2019, p. 28).
Building on Others' Contributions
The most productive seminars feature cumulative argumentation — each speaker builds on, qualifies, or extends what the previous speaker said, rather than simply waiting for a turn to speak independently (Oxford, 2018, p. 22).
Conciseness
Academic oral contributions should be focused. Make one clear point, support it briefly with evidence or reasoning, and stop. Avoid rambling, repetition, or personal anecdote disguised as academic argument (Bailey, 2018, p. 82).
Section IV

Academic Discourse Markers and Phrases

Academic speakers and writers use discourse markers — words and phrases that signal the logical relationship between ideas — to organise contributions clearly and signal their communicative intent to the audience or reader (Hyland, 2019, p. 34). Mastering these phrases is one of the fastest ways to raise the register and coherence of both spoken and written academic English (Swales & Feak, 2020, p. 25).

Function
Academic Phrases
Introducing a point
"To begin with…," "The first point to consider is…," "It should be noted that…"
Adding information
"Furthermore…," "In addition to this…," "It is also worth noting that…"
Contrasting
"However…," "On the other hand…," "Notwithstanding this…," "In contrast…"
Conceding a point
"While it is true that…," "Granted that…," "Although one might argue…"
Giving evidence
"The data indicates…," "As Smith (2021) demonstrates…," "Evidence suggests that…"
Disagreeing respectfully
"That position may overlook…," "With respect, this argument does not account for…"
Summarising
"In summary…," "To recapitulate…," "The foregoing analysis suggests…"
Concluding
"In conclusion…," "It may therefore be argued…," "The evidence leads to the conclusion that…"
Section V

Practical Activity: Register and Discourse

✏️
Practical Activity
Integrated Language Use — 10 Items

For items 1–5, rewrite the informal expression in formal academic register. For items 6–10, identify the discourse function of the phrase or sentence and name the appropriate discourse marker that could introduce it.

  • 1
    "Everyone knows that corruption is bad for Nigeria."
    Remove absolute claim — rewrite with evidence and hedging.
    Formalise
  • 2
    "I think the study's results are kind of wrong."
    Replace "I think" and "kind of" with academic equivalents.
    Formalise
  • 3
    "The government should do a lot of stuff about poverty."
    Replace colloquial terms with precise academic vocabulary.
    Formalise
  • 4
    "You're wrong — the policy didn't work at all."
    Rewrite to disagree respectfully using impersonal construction.
    Formalise
  • 5
    "It's pretty clear that literacy rates affect development."
    Remove contraction, hedge appropriately, add evidence signal.
    Formalise
  • 6
    You want to introduce an opposing argument while conceding its partial validity.
    Which phrase signals concession before rebuttal?
    Concession Marker
  • 7
    You have made three points and want to bring your seminar contribution to a close.
    Which phrase signals summary or conclusion?
    Conclusion Marker
  • 8
    A peer makes a good argument. You want to build on it and add a further point.
    Which phrase allows you to add without dismissing?
    Addition Marker
  • 9
    You want to cite a researcher's specific finding as evidence for your claim.
    Which phrase introduces reported evidence appropriately?
    Evidence Marker
  • 10
    A student in a seminar speaks for eight minutes, repeating the same point multiple times and ignoring others' contributions. Which seminar participation principle is violated?
    Name the principle and explain why it matters.
    Conciseness & Listening
Section VI

Conclusion & Module 3 Summary

Language skills are not compartments — they are integrated, interdependent, and mutually reinforcing. A student who reads strategically brings richer content to discussions. A student who listens actively gives more relevant responses. A student who speaks with clarity and appropriate register is more persuasive. A student who writes with precision and formal vocabulary thinks more clearly about complex ideas (Oxford, 2018, p. 28).


The goal of this module is not to make students perform language but to make them use language — with purpose, awareness, and increasing sophistication — in every academic context they encounter across their university career (Nation & Newton, 2019, p. 45).
