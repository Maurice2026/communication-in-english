Suprasegmentals — Stress and Intonation

⏱ Duration: 25 Minutes 📚 Module: Phonetics & Phonology 🎯 Level: Tertiary — New Entrants ✅ Status: Live
Section I

What Are Suprasegmentals?

Lectures 1.1 and 1.2 focused on the segments of speech — the individual vowels and consonants that make up words. This lecture turns to the features that operate above and across those segments: the suprasegmentals. These are the prosodic properties of speech — stress, intonation, tone, rhythm, and tempo — that function at the level of the syllable, word, phrase, and entire utterance (Cruttenden, 2021, p. 250).

A student may pronounce every individual sound perfectly, yet still be misunderstood if stress is placed on the wrong syllable or if intonation conveys the wrong communicative intent (Jenkins, 2022, p. 11). Suprasegmentals carry meaning independently of phonemes — they are the music of the language (Roach, 2022, p. 88).

Key Concept
Suprasegmentals
Phonological features that extend over more than one sound segment — including word stress, sentence stress, intonation, rhythm, and tempo. They modify and add meaning to the segmental content of utterances (Ladefoged & Johnson, 2020, p. 231).
Prosody
The broader term for the study of the rhythm and melody of speech, encompassing all suprasegmental features (Skandera & Burleigh, 2019, p. 68).
Section II

Word Stress — The Heartbeat of the Word

In English, every word with more than one syllable has one syllable that receives greater prominence — it is spoken with greater loudness, length, and pitch height than the others (Roach, 2022, p. 90). This is the stressed syllable. In IPA transcription, stress is marked by a small vertical line ˈ before the stressed syllable: photograph → /ˈfəʊtəɡrɑːf/.

The Stress-Shift Rule: Noun vs. Verb Pairs

One of the most academically significant stress patterns in English is the noun-verb stress shift — where the same word functions as either a noun or a verb depending on which syllable carries the stress (Cruttenden, 2021, p. 262). This is particularly relevant in academic and formal professional English:

Sentence Stress: Content vs. Structure Words

English is a stress-timed language — its rhythm is created by the regular recurrence of stressed syllables, regardless of how many unstressed syllables fall between them (Skandera & Burleigh, 2019, p. 72). This means certain words in a sentence are stressed while others are reduced.

Content Words vs. Structure Words
Content Words — Stressed
Nouns, main verbs, adjectives, and adverbs. They carry the core meaning of the sentence and receive stress: students, reading, library.
Structure Words — Unstressed
Articles (a, the), prepositions (in, at, of), pronouns (he, she, it), and conjunctions (and, but). These are grammatical glue — typically reduced to the schwa /ə/ in fluent speech (Deterding, 2018, p. 322).

Example: "The students are reading in the library." In natural speech, the /ðə/, are /ə/, and in /ɪn/ are weakened and spoken rapidly, while students, reading, and library carry full stress (Aremo, 2021, p. 517).

Section III

Intonation — The Melody of Meaning

Intonation is the systematic rise and fall of pitch across an utterance. It is not random — in English, specific intonation patterns carry specific communicative meanings, signalling whether the speaker has finished speaking, is asking a question, making a request, or expressing uncertainty (Roach, 2022, p. 107).

Section IV

Practical Activity: Stress Shift and Meaning

One of the most striking demonstrations of how suprasegmentals carry independent meaning is the stress-shift exercise. Read the same sentence multiple times, each time placing the primary stress on a different word. The change in stress shifts the implied meaning — even though every phoneme stays identical (Skandera & Burleigh, 2019, p. 76).

Core Sentence Activity
"I didn't say she stole the money."
  • 1I didn't say she stole the money.
    Implied meaning: Someone else said it — not me.
  • 2I didn't say she stole the money.
    Implied meaning: Strong denial — I absolutely did not say it.
  • 3I didn't say she stole the money.
    Implied meaning: I implied it or wrote it — but did not say it aloud.
  • 4I didn't say she stole the money.
    Implied meaning: I said someone else stole it — not specifically her.
  • 5I didn't say she stole the money.
    Implied meaning: She may have borrowed it — the method is in question.
  • 6I didn't say she stole the money.
    Implied meaning: She stole something else, or some (unspecified) money.
  • 7I didn't say she stole the money.
    Implied meaning: She stole something — but not specifically money.

Section V

Conclusion & Key Takeaway

A student may pronounce every phoneme flawlessly, yet still fail to communicate effectively if stress and intonation are misplaced. Suprasegmentals are not decorative features of speech — they are meaning-bearing at every level of utterance (Jenkins, 2022, p. 15).


Mastering the stress-shift rule, recognising content and structure words, and controlling falling and rising intonation patterns will make a direct and measurable difference to performance in academic presentations, seminars, viva examinations, and professional communication (Aremo, 2021, p. 520).
