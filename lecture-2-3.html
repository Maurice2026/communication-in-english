Section I

What is Concord?

Among the most common grammatical errors in Nigerian undergraduate writing is the violation of concord — also called agreement. Concord is the grammatical requirement that certain elements within a sentence must match each other in number, person, or gender (Huddleston & Pullum, 2019, p. 130). The most critical form of concord in English is subject-verb agreement: the verb must reflect whether its subject is singular or plural (Iwuchukwu, 2020, p. 50).

Concord errors do not merely mark a student as grammatically weak — in formal academic and professional writing, they undermine the credibility of the entire argument. A sentence that lacks concord signals inattention to the conventions of standard written English (Oladele, 2021, p. 119).

Key Concept
Concord (Agreement)
The grammatical relationship in which two or more words in a sentence must match in form — typically in number (singular/plural) or person (first/second/third) (Biber et al., 2021, p. 185).
The Golden Rule of Subject-Verb Concord
A singular subject takes a singular verb; a plural subject takes a plural verb. This rule appears simple but becomes complex in several high-frequency academic constructions (Quirk et al., 2018, p. 102).
Section II

Subject-Verb Concord — Core Rules and Problem Areas

The basic rule is straightforward. It is its application in complex grammatical environments that creates difficulty. The following are the most important concord rules for academic writing (Quirk et al., 2018, p. 105):

Rule 1 — Basic Agreement
A singular subject takes a singular verb; a plural subject takes a plural verb.
✓ "The student writes clearly."  |  ✓ "The students write clearly."
Rule 2 — Intervening Phrases
A phrase between the subject and verb does not change the number of the subject. The verb must agree with the head of the subject noun phrase, not the nearest noun (Huddleston & Pullum, 2019, p. 133).
✗ "The quality of the reports are poor."  |  ✓ "The quality of the reports is poor." (Subject = quality, singular)
Rule 3 — Either/Or and Neither/Nor
When subjects are joined by either…or or neither…nor, the verb agrees with the subject closest to it (the proximity rule) (Biber et al., 2021, p. 192).
✓ "Either the dean or the lecturers are responsible."  |  ✓ "Either the lecturers or the dean is responsible."
Rule 4 — Collective Nouns
Collective nouns (committee, team, government, jury, staff, audience) take a singular verb when the group acts as a unified whole, and a plural verb when individual members act independently. In Nigerian academic English, the singular form is standard (Quirk et al., 2018, p. 110).
✓ "The committee has reached a decision." (acting as one)  |  "The committee are divided on the issue." (British English — members acting individually)
Rule 5 — Indefinite Pronouns
Pronouns such as everyone, nobody, each, either, neither, someone, anyone are grammatically singular and always take a singular verb, even when their sense is plural (Huddleston & Pullum, 2019, p. 140).
✗ "Everyone have submitted their forms."  |  ✓ "Everyone has submitted their forms."
Rule 6 — Subjects Joined by "and"
Two or more subjects joined by and form a compound subject and take a plural verb — unless they refer to the same entity or are treated as a unit (Biber et al., 2021, p. 189).
✓ "The rector and the registrar have signed the document."  |  ✓ "Bread and butter is her favourite breakfast." (single unit)

Section III

Pronoun-Antecedent Agreement

A second form of concord that demands attention in academic writing is pronoun-antecedent agreement. A pronoun must agree in number and person with the noun it refers back to — its antecedent (Crystal, 2019, p. 115). Violations of this rule are frequent in student writing and often create ambiguity about who or what is being discussed (Oladele, 2021, p. 122).

Rule 7 — Pronoun-Antecedent Number Agreement
A singular antecedent takes a singular pronoun; a plural antecedent takes a plural pronoun (Biber et al., 2021, p. 200).
✗ "Every student must submit their assignment." (singular antecedent, plural pronoun — grammatically problematic in formal writing)  |  ✓ "Every student must submit his or her assignment." (formal) or restructure: ✓ "All students must submit their assignments."
Rule 8 — Clear Antecedent Reference
Every pronoun must have a clear, unambiguous antecedent. When two nouns precede a pronoun, the pronoun's referent must be immediately clear (Huddleston & Pullum, 2019, p. 145).
✗ "The dean met the lecturer and he submitted the report." (Who submitted — the dean or the lecturer?)  |  ✓ "The dean met the lecturer, who then submitted the report."
📌

Academic Writing Rule: When concord is in doubt — particularly with collective nouns or indefinite pronouns — restructure the sentence to avoid the problem entirely. Clarity is always preferable to a technically correct but awkward construction (Swales & Feak, 2020, p. 58).

Section V

Conclusion & Key Takeaway

Concord is not a peripheral grammatical concern — it is the very mechanism by which English signals the logical relationships between subjects and their actions, and between pronouns and the entities they represent. Every violation of concord introduces ambiguity or inaccuracy into a text (Biber et al., 2021, p. 205).


The eight rules covered in this lecture address the vast majority of concord errors found in Nigerian undergraduate writing. A student who masters these rules — particularly the intervening phrase rule, the collective noun rule, and the indefinite pronoun rule — will eliminate the most persistent source of grammatical error in their academic essays (Iwuchukwu, 2020, p. 60).
