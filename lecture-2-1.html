Section I

Introduction: From Sound to Structure

Module 1 established the sound system of English — the 44 phonemes, the organs that produce them, and the suprasegmental features that give speech its rhythm and meaning. Module 2 now turns to a different but equally fundamental dimension of language: grammar — the structural system that governs how words are organised into meaningful sentences (Biber et al., 2021, p. 3).

The starting point for any study of grammar is the word class — sometimes called a part of speech. Every word in English belongs to a class that determines how it behaves grammatically: where it can appear in a sentence, what forms it can take, and what kind of meaning it contributes (Huddleston & Pullum, 2019, p. 17). A student who cannot identify word classes cannot diagnose or correct grammatical errors in their own writing — a critical skill in academic and professional contexts (Yule, 2020, p. 71).

Key Concept
Word Class (Part of Speech)
A category of words that share grammatical properties — the same syntactic functions, the same morphological possibilities, and the same distributional patterns in sentences (Huddleston & Pullum, 2019, p. 18).
Open vs. Closed Classes
English word classes divide into two major groups: open classes — which regularly admit new members — and closed classes — whose membership is fixed and rarely changes (Biber et al., 2021, p. 56).
Section II

Open Classes — The Meaning Carriers

Open classes are called "open" because new words are continuously added to them as language evolves. They carry the primary lexical and referential meaning of sentences (Crystal, 2019, p. 84). English has four major open classes:

Word ClassDefinitionExamplesTest
Noun Names a person, place, thing, concept, or idea. Can be preceded by a determiner (the, a) (Yule, 2020, p. 72). student, university, justice, democracy, Lagos Can follow the: the student ✓
Verb Expresses an action, event, process, or state. Changes form to indicate tense, aspect, and agreement (Biber et al., 2021, p. 68). write, analyse, become, present, conduct Can follow will: will write ✓
Adjective Modifies a noun, attributing a quality, quantity, or characteristic to it (Huddleston & Pullum, 2019, p. 52). academic, comprehensive, critical, formal, precise Fits: very __ : very academic ✓
Adverb Modifies a verb, adjective, another adverb, or an entire clause, typically expressing manner, time, degree, or frequency (Crystal, 2019, p. 92). quickly, thoroughly, however, therefore, very Modifies the verb: writes clearly ✓
📌

Academic Note: New words entering English almost always join one of the open classes — especially nouns and verbs. Words like googling (verb), selfie (noun), and viral (adjective) are recent additions. Closed classes, by contrast, have not gained significant new members in centuries (Biber et al., 2021, p. 57).

Section III

Closed Classes — The Grammatical Glue

Closed classes contain a fixed, limited set of words that serve primarily grammatical — rather than lexical — functions. They bind the open-class words together into coherent sentences (Quirk et al., 2018, p. 67). While individual members are few, they are among the most frequently used words in the language.

Word ClassDefinitionCommon Members
Pronoun Substitutes for a noun or noun phrase to avoid repetition (Yule, 2020, p. 76). I, you, he, she, it, we, they, who, which, this, that
Determiner Introduces a noun phrase, specifying reference or quantity (Huddleston & Pullum, 2019, p. 60). the, a, an, this, that, these, those, my, your, every, some
Preposition Shows the relationship between a noun/pronoun and another element in the sentence (Crystal, 2019, p. 97). in, on, at, of, by, for, with, through, between, during
Conjunction Joins words, phrases, or clauses. Co-ordinating conjunctions join equals; subordinating conjunctions introduce dependent clauses (Biber et al., 2021, p. 80). and, but, or, nor, yet, so, for, because, although, when, if
Auxiliary Verb Helps the main verb express tense, aspect, modality, or voice (Quirk et al., 2018, p. 74). be, have, do, will, would, shall, should, may, might, must, can
Section IV

Functional Shifts — When Words Change Class

One of the most important and frequently misunderstood features of English grammar is functional shift — also called conversion or zero derivation. This is the process by which a word moves from one word class to another without any change in its form (Bauer et al., 2018, p. 143). The word's class is determined entirely by its function in the sentence — not by its spelling or sound (Huddleston & Pullum, 2019, p. 22).

Noun → Verb
NounWe need a Google search. (Google = company name)
VerbLet me Google that for you. (Google = to search)
NounHe gave a report on the findings.
VerbShe will report to the committee.
Adjective → Noun
AdjectiveThe poor performance was noted.
NounThe government must protect the poor.
AdjectiveA daily exercise routine is recommended.
NounThe daily arrived at seven o'clock.
Verb → Noun
VerbWe must walk to the venue immediately.
NounThe evening walk was very refreshing.
VerbPlease run the programme again.
NounThe candidate had a long run of victories.
Noun → Adjective
NounShe works in gold and other precious metals.
AdjectiveHe received a gold medal for his research.
NounThe stone was found near the riverbank.
AdjectiveThe stone building withstood the flood.

The grammatical class of a word can only be determined by studying its role in the sentence — not by looking at the word in isolation. This principle is foundational to accurate grammatical analysis (Biber et al., 2021, p. 62).

Section VI

Conclusion & Key Takeaway

Word classes are not merely a labelling exercise — they are the analytical framework through which grammar is understood and applied. Knowing that a word is a noun tells you it can be the subject of a clause, can be pluralised, and can be preceded by a determiner. Knowing that a word is an adverb tells you it can be moved to different positions in the sentence without changing core meaning (Yule, 2020, p. 78).


The concept of functional shift is particularly important in academic writing, where the same word may appear as a noun in one sentence and a verb in the next. Misidentifying the class leads to errors in agreement, tense, and sentence structure — the precise errors this module is designed to eliminate (Huddleston & Pullum, 2019, p. 25).
