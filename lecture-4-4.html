Section I

What Is a CV?

A Curriculum Vitae (from the Latin, meaning "course of life") is a comprehensive written document that presents a person’s educational background, professional experience, skills, achievements, and qualifications. It is the primary document used in the Nigerian job market and in academic applications to introduce oneself to potential employers, institutions, or selection committees (Paltridge & Starfield, 2020, p. 112).

The CV is not merely a list — it is a carefully curated, strategically organised argument that the applicant is the best candidate for the position being applied for. Every section, every bullet point, and every word choice should serve the purpose of convincing the reader that this particular person possesses exactly the qualifications, experience, and character the role requires.

In the Nigerian context, a well-written CV is often the only representation of a candidate before an interview is offered. Employers and human resources departments may receive dozens or hundreds of CVs for a single position. A CV that is poorly formatted, grammatically incorrect, inappropriately lengthy, or missing key information will be eliminated without the candidate ever knowing. Understanding what makes a CV excellent is therefore a career-critical skill (Andrews & Smith, 2019, p. 88).

CV vs. Résumé — An Important Distinction
Curriculum Vitae (CV)
A comprehensive document presenting the full record of a person’s academic and professional life. In Nigeria and the UK, CVs are typically 2–4 pages (longer for senior academics or professionals). A CV includes all qualifications, publications, conferences, professional memberships, and detailed experience. Used for most professional and academic applications in Nigeria (Paltridge & Starfield, 2020, p. 114).
Résumé
A shorter (1–2 page) document common in the United States and Canada, tailored specifically to one job application. It includes only the most relevant experience for that particular role, omitting anything not directly applicable. The résumé is less common in Nigeria but is increasingly used for international applications and positions with global companies. The principles of good writing apply equally to both.
Section II

Types of CV

The three main types of CV serve different needs depending on your career stage, career history, and the nature of the role being applied for (Hacker & Sommers, 2021, p. 224).

TypeStructureBest ForNigerian Application
Chronological CV Experience listed in reverse chronological order (most recent first). The standard and most widely used format. Candidates with a consistent, relevant work history. Employers can quickly see career progression and the most recent experience. The most common format used in Nigerian professional applications. Recommended for graduates and experienced professionals alike unless there are specific gaps or career changes to manage.
Functional CV Skills and competencies are listed first and emphasised, with less detail on the chronology of employment. Work history is present but de-emphasised. Career changers, those with employment gaps, or candidates whose skills are more impressive than their job titles suggest. Less common in Nigeria but useful for graduates applying for roles in a field different from their degree discipline, or for those returning to work after a gap.
Combined CV Begins with a skills summary, then presents a full chronological work history. Combines the advantages of both formats. Experienced professionals who want to highlight transferable skills while also demonstrating a strong career record. Increasingly used by Nigerian mid-career professionals applying for senior positions. Also useful for academic applications where both research skills and employment history matter.
📌

For Nigerian University Graduates: The chronological CV is the recommended format for most first-job applications. It is familiar to Nigerian employers, easy to read, and clearly presents your educational qualifications — which, as a recent graduate, are your primary asset. Ensure your most recent degree and most relevant academic achievements appear prominently (Andrews & Smith, 2019, p. 92).

Section III

Sections of a Complete Professional CV

A complete, professional Nigerian CV typically contains the following sections, in this order. Each section serves a distinct purpose and should be presented with careful attention to formatting, accuracy, and relevance (Oshima & Hogue, 2021, p. 198).

The Eight Essential Sections of a Nigerian Professional CV
1. Personal Information / Contact Details
Full name (in bold, slightly larger font), phone number, professional email address, city and state of residence, and optionally a LinkedIn URL. Do not include date of birth, religion, ethnicity, marital status, or a photograph unless specifically requested — these details are not required in a modern professional CV and may expose you to discrimination. Your email address must be professional (e.g., a.okonkwo@gmail.com, not "fancyboy2002@yahoo.com").
2. Personal Statement / Professional Summary
A concise paragraph of 3–5 sentences summarising who you are professionally, your key qualifications, and what you bring to the role. This replaces the old-fashioned "Objective" section. It should be tailored to the specific job and should immediately make the employer want to read more. It answers: who are you? what can you do? what are you seeking? (Hacker & Sommers, 2021, p. 228).
3. Education
Listed in reverse chronological order (most recent first). Include: name of institution, degree awarded, year of graduation, and class of degree or CGPA if strong. Include secondary school (WAEC/NECO) details if you are a recent graduate. Include NYSC completion year. Do not include primary school.
4. Work Experience / Professional Experience
Listed in reverse chronological order. For each role: job title, name of organisation, dates of employment (month and year), and 3–5 bullet points describing your responsibilities and achievements. Use action verbs: "Coordinated...", "Developed...", "Managed...", "Analysed...", "Facilitated...". Quantify achievements where possible: "Managed a team of 8 staff" is stronger than "Managed staff."
5. Skills
A concise list of relevant professional and technical skills: software proficiency, languages spoken, research methods, project management tools, laboratory techniques (if applicable). Distinguish between proficiency levels where relevant. Only list skills you genuinely possess — interviewers frequently test claimed skills directly.
6. Professional Memberships and Certifications
Membership of relevant professional bodies (NMA, NBA, NSE, NUJ, ICAN, CIPM, etc.), relevant professional certifications (PMP, Google Analytics, IELTS, etc.), and any awards or honours received. Include the year obtained.
7. Publications and Research (if applicable)
For academic and research positions, list any published articles, conference papers, research projects, or dissertations in the appropriate citation format. For non-academic applications, this section may be omitted or replaced with "Projects."
8. Referees
List two or three referees who can speak to your professional or academic competence. Always seek permission before listing anyone as a referee. Include their name, title, organisation, and contact details. "References available upon request" is acceptable in some contexts but listing named referees is stronger. Nigerian employers typically expect referees to be included.

Section V

The Application / Cover Letter

An application letter (also called a cover letter) is a formal letter submitted alongside your CV when applying for a job, postgraduate programme, scholarship, or other opportunity. While the CV lists your qualifications and experience, the application letter tells your story — it explains why you are applying, why you are the right person, and how your specific background connects to this specific opportunity. A generic application letter that could have been sent to any employer is as bad as no letter at all (Hyland, 2019, p. 156).

Structure of a Professional Application Letter — Four Paragraphs
Paragraph 1 — Opening: State Your Purpose
Clearly state the position you are applying for, where you saw the advertisement (or how you learned of the opportunity), and a brief, compelling statement of why you are interested. Be specific about the role and the organisation. "I wish to apply for the position of Communications Officer advertised in The Punch newspaper of 12 April 2026" is professional. "I am writing to express my interest in working for your esteemed organisation" is vague and unimpressive.
Paragraph 2 — Education and Relevant Qualifications
Highlight your most relevant educational qualifications — not as a repeat of your CV, but as a focused argument for your suitability. Mention your degree, class, institution, and any particularly relevant coursework, research, or projects. Connect your academic background directly to the requirements of the role: "My final-year project on digital media strategy directly addresses the content development responsibilities outlined in your job description."
Paragraph 3 — Experience and Demonstrable Skills
Present your most relevant professional or practical experience as evidence of your ability to perform the job. Describe one or two specific achievements or responsibilities that directly align with what the employer is seeking. This paragraph should make the employer feel that you already understand the role and have demonstrated competence in similar work. Use quantified achievements where possible (Paltridge & Starfield, 2020, p. 120).
Paragraph 4 — Closing: Call to Action
Express enthusiasm for the role, indicate that your CV is enclosed/attached for further review, and request an interview or further discussion. Provide your availability and preferred contact method. Thank the reader for their time and consideration. End with an appropriate formal sign-off.

The letter is four substantive paragraphs — approximately one page — the appropriate length for an application letter.
Section VI

CV and Application Writing — Do’s and Don’ts

✓ CV and Application Do’s
  • Tailor both CV and cover letter specifically to each job and each employer — generic applications are immediately apparent and rarely succeed
  • Use a clean, professional font (Calibri, Garamond, Times New Roman) in 11–12pt; ensure consistent formatting throughout
  • Use strong action verbs at the start of every experience bullet: Developed, Led, Managed, Coordinated, Analysed, Designed, Achieved
  • Quantify achievements wherever possible: numbers, percentages, team sizes, budgets managed, outcomes achieved
  • Proofread meticulously: a single spelling error can eliminate an otherwise excellent candidate
  • Save and submit in PDF format to preserve formatting across all devices and operating systems
  • Keep your CV to 2–3 pages as a recent graduate; 3–4 pages for mid-career professionals
  • Use consistent date formats throughout (e.g., "Aug 2022 — Jul 2023" throughout, not mixed with "2022/2023")
✗ CV and Application Don’ts
  • Do not include photograph, religion, ethnicity, tribe, or marital status unless specifically required by the employer
  • Do not use "Objective" statements (“My objective is to obtain a challenging position...”) — replace with a professional summary
  • Do not use informal or unprofessional language anywhere in either document
  • Do not list referees without their prior knowledge and permission
  • Do not exaggerate or falsify qualifications, grades, or experience — these claims are verified, and discovered dishonesty ends careers
  • Do not submit a cover letter that begins with “I am writing to humbly apply...” — the word "humbly" is inappropriate in professional applications
  • Do not use dense paragraphs in the experience section — use bullet points for clarity and scannability
  • Do not include irrelevant personal hobbies unless they directly demonstrate relevant skills (e.g., "Team Captain, University Football Club" demonstrates leadership)

Practical Writing Activity — Section VII
CV and Application Letter Writing Tasks

Complete the following writing tasks. All responses must follow the professional standards and structures taught in this lecture.

  • 1Write your own complete chronological CV using the model in this lecture as a guide. Include all eight sections. Your CV should be 2–3 pages and tailored to your chosen career field. Submit for peer review — each student should review one classmate’s CV and provide written feedback on structure, content, language, and formatting.
  • 2Find a real job advertisement posted by a Nigerian company on Jobberman, LinkedIn, or any credible recruitment platform. Write a 4-paragraph application letter responding to that specific advertisement, using the model in this lecture as your guide. The letter must be tailored to the specific role and organisation — generic letters will not be accepted.
  • 3Rewrite the following weak experience bullet point as two strong, quantified, action-verb-led bullet points: "I was responsible for helping with the social media of the company and also I did some writing and helped to plan events during my time there."
  • 4A student writes this personal statement: "My objective is to work in a good company where I can grow and contribute to the organisation." Rewrite this as a professional summary of 3–5 sentences for a graduate applying for a banking role, using the model in this lecture as your guide.
  • 5Read the following application letter opening and identify at least FIVE specific problems with it. Then rewrite the opening paragraph professionally: "Dear Sir/Ma, I am humbly writing this letter to inform you of my desire to work in your very prestigious and esteemed organisation. I am a graduate and I have experience and I believe I can add value if given the opportunity."
Conclusion

Key Takeaway

Your CV and application letter are the first impression you make on a potential employer — and in the Nigerian job market, where competition is intense and opportunities are precious, that first impression may be your only one. A well-crafted CV communicates not just your qualifications but your professionalism, your attention to detail, your communication ability, and your seriousness of purpose. These are qualities every employer seeks (Andrews & Smith, 2019, p. 100).
