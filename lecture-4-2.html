Section I

The Thesis Statement

The thesis statement is the single most important sentence in an academic essay. It is the controlling idea that tells the reader what the essay will argue, how it will be structured, and what position the writer takes. Every sentence in a well-written essay ultimately serves the thesis (Lunsford, 2021, p. 44).

A thesis statement is not a simple statement of fact, a question, or a vague expression of intention. It is a specific, arguable claim that requires evidence and reasoning to support. Compare the following:

Weak vs. Strong Thesis Statements

Weak: "Social media has changed communication in Nigeria." (Too vague — this is a statement of fact, not an arguable claim. Everyone agrees with it.)

Strong: "While social media platforms have expanded access to information for Nigerian youth, their uncritical adoption has undermined the quality of public discourse by privileging speed and emotional reaction over accuracy and deliberate reasoning."

Why the strong thesis works: It takes a specific, nuanced position. It acknowledges a counterpoint (expanded access) before asserting its main claim (undermined discourse quality). It identifies specific mechanisms (speed over accuracy, emotion over reasoning). A reader immediately knows what the essay will argue and what evidence it will need to support the claim.
Characteristics of an Effective Thesis Statement
Specific and Arguable
A thesis must make a claim that can be disputed. If everyone already agrees with the statement, it is a fact, not an argument. Ask yourself: could a reasonable person disagree with this? If not, sharpen the claim (Graff & Birkenstein, 2021, p. 22).
Focused and Appropriately Scoped
The thesis must be narrow enough to be addressed within the word limit of the essay. An essay of 1,000 words cannot adequately argue "Nigeria’s economy should be reformed." It could argue "The Nigerian government should prioritise investment in agricultural infrastructure over oil sector expansion as the primary driver of economic recovery."
Positioned Correctly
In most academic essays, the thesis appears at the end of the introduction — after the hook and the contextualising background, and before the first body paragraph. This placement signals the reader that the groundwork has been laid and the argument is about to begin (Oshima & Hogue, 2020, p. 38).
Supported by the Body
Every main point in the body of the essay must directly support or develop the thesis. If a body paragraph does not serve the thesis, it should be cut or revised. The thesis is the test that every paragraph must pass.
Section II

The PEEL Paragraph Method

PEEL is a widely used framework for constructing well-developed academic body paragraphs. It ensures that every paragraph is logically structured, evidenced, and clearly connected to the thesis (Bailey, 2021, p. 68). Each letter stands for a key component:

The PEEL Framework
P — Point
The topic sentence of the paragraph — the single main idea this paragraph will discuss. It should be clearly connected to the thesis and introduce the argument the paragraph will make. It is not a statement of fact; it is a mini-argument that the paragraph will then prove. Example: "The proliferation of unverified information on WhatsApp has made it increasingly difficult for Nigerian citizens to distinguish credible news from deliberate misinformation."
E — Evidence
The specific support for the Point — statistics, research findings, expert opinion, case studies, or examples. Evidence must come from credible sources and must be cited. Evidence alone is not enough: you must show how it proves your point. Example: "A 2022 study by Dubawa found that 68% of viral health-related messages circulating on Nigerian WhatsApp groups contained factually inaccurate information (Adeyemi & Nwosu, 2022, p. 14)."
E — Explanation
Your analysis of the evidence — the reasoning that connects the evidence to the Point and to the thesis. This is where your own academic voice is most clearly heard. Do not let evidence speak for itself; explain what it means, why it matters, and how it supports your argument. Example: "This finding indicates that the informal, unregulated nature of peer-to-peer messaging platforms creates environments where misinformation spreads faster than correction."
L — Link
A closing sentence that connects the paragraph back to the thesis and/or transitions to the next paragraph. The Link prevents paragraphs from being isolated units and maintains the logical flow of the argument. Example: "This erosion of information credibility therefore directly undermines the quality of public discourse that a healthy democracy depends upon, a pattern that becomes even more pronounced in electoral periods — as the next section will demonstrate."
Complete PEEL Paragraph — Annotated Example

[POINT] The adoption of social media has significantly expanded access to information for Nigerians who were previously excluded from formal news media. [EVIDENCE] According to the Nigeria Communications Commission (2023), mobile internet penetration reached 47.8% in 2022, with social media being the primary mode of news consumption for citizens in rural and semi-urban areas. A study by Nwosu and Emeka (2022, p. 33) found that 72% of respondents in rural Anambra State received their first news of major national events through WhatsApp before accessing any formal news outlet. [EXPLANATION] These figures demonstrate that social media platforms have effectively democratised information access, enabling citizens to participate in national conversations from which geographic and economic barriers had previously excluded them. [LINK] This increased access, however, carries a significant qualification: the same channels that distribute genuine news also propagate misinformation with equal efficiency, raising serious questions about the net quality of digital information exposure — questions that the following section addresses directly.

Analysis: Notice how each element does its work. The Point introduces a specific, arguable claim. The Evidence provides two specific, cited data points. The Explanation interprets the evidence — it doesn’t just repeat what the evidence says, it analyses what it means. The Link acknowledges the complexity and signals the next section, maintaining argumentative momentum.
Section III

Cohesion and Transitional Expressions

Cohesion is the quality that makes a text read as a unified, connected whole rather than a collection of isolated sentences and paragraphs. Academic essays must be cohesive at two levels: within paragraphs (how sentences connect to each other) and across paragraphs (how paragraphs connect to each other and to the thesis) (Halliday & Matthiessen, 2019, p. 310).

FunctionTransitional ExpressionsExample in Academic Writing
Adding a pointFurthermore, Moreover, In addition, Additionally, Also"Furthermore, this pattern is not unique to urban centres; rural communities show even higher rates of social media reliance for news."
ContrastingHowever, Nevertheless, On the other hand, Conversely, Yet, Despite this"However, this expansion of access does not automatically translate into improved information quality."
Cause and effectTherefore, Thus, Consequently, As a result, Hence, This leads to"Consequently, the credibility of digital news sources has become a major concern for researchers and policymakers."
IllustratingFor example, For instance, To illustrate, Specifically, In particular"For instance, a viral message claiming that lemon juice could cure malaria spread to over 2 million Nigerian WhatsApp users within 48 hours."
Conceding a pointAdmittedly, It is true that, While it may be argued that, Although"Admittedly, social media platforms have implemented fact-checking tools; however, their effectiveness in the Nigerian context remains limited."
ConcludingIn conclusion, To summarise, Overall, In sum, This demonstrates that"In conclusion, the evidence presented indicates that the net effect of social media on Nigerian public discourse is more ambiguous than either its advocates or critics typically acknowledge."
📌

Caution on Overuse: Transitional expressions are powerful but must be used with precision. Using them incorrectly — writing "Therefore" when the relationship is actually one of addition, or "However" when there is no genuine contrast — confuses the reader and weakens the argument. Use them only when the logical relationship they signal actually exists between the ideas being connected (Hacker & Sommers, 2021, p. 88).

Section IV

Integrating and Citing Sources

Academic essays draw on the work of other scholars to support, contextualise, and develop their arguments. The skill of integrating sources effectively — using them to strengthen your own argument without allowing them to dominate or replace your own voice — is a mark of academic maturity (Hyland, 2019, p. 55).

Three Ways to Integrate a Source

1. Direct Quotation: Use the author’s exact words, enclosed in quotation marks, with a full citation. Use sparingly — only when the exact wording is important. Example: According to Nwosu (2021, p. 45), "digital misinformation in Nigeria operates through a complex network of trust relationships that existing fact-checking models fail to account for."

2. Paraphrase: Restate the source’s ideas in your own words and sentence structure, with a full citation. This is the most common and preferred form of source integration. Shows you understand the material, not just copied it.

3. Summary: Condense the main argument or finding of a longer text in your own words, with a citation. Useful for providing background or referencing a general argument without detailed discussion.

APA 7th Edition — Citation Basics

In-text citation (narrative): Nwosu (2021, p. 45) argues that...

In-text citation (parenthetical): This pattern has been observed across multiple studies (Nwosu, 2021; Adeyemi & Obi, 2022).

Two authors: Adeyemi and Obi (2022) found that... / (Adeyemi & Obi, 2022)

Three or more authors: Bassham et al. (2020) argue... / (Bassham et al., 2020)

Reference list entry (journal article):
Nwosu, C. E. (2021). Title of article. Journal Name, volume(issue), page–range. https://doi.org/xxxxx

Reference list entry (book):
Author, A. A. (Year). Title of work. Publisher.

📌

The Voice Balance Rule: Your own analytical voice should dominate the essay. Sources support your argument — they do not make it for you. If you find that more than 20–25% of any paragraph is direct quotation or close paraphrase of a single source, you have lost your own voice. Integrate, analyse, and move forward. The essay must be yours (Lunsford, 2021, p. 66).

Section V

Writing Introductions and Conclusions

The introduction and conclusion are the first and last things a reader encounters — they shape the overall impression of the essay and determine whether the argument feels purposeful and complete (Andrews & Smith, 2018, p. 92).

The Introduction — Structure and Function
Hook (Opening Sentence or Two)
A compelling opening that engages the reader and establishes relevance. Options include: a striking statistic, a thought-provoking question, a brief relevant anecdote, or a bold assertion. The hook should be directly related to the essay topic — not a generic statement. Avoid beginning with "Since the dawn of time..." or "In today’s world..." — these are clichéd and weak.
Context and Background
Two to four sentences that provide the reader with essential background information needed to understand the thesis — definitions of key terms, the current state of the debate, relevant historical context, or the scope of the problem. This should narrow progressively from broad to specific, leading the reader toward the thesis.
Thesis Statement
The final sentence (or occasionally two sentences) of the introduction. This is your controlling argument. By the time the reader reaches the thesis, they should feel that it is the inevitable conclusion of the funnel the introduction has created.
The Conclusion — Structure and Function
Restatement of Thesis (Not Repetition)
Begin by restating the thesis in different words, showing how the body of the essay has now established it rather than merely asserted it. The thesis at the end of the essay should feel proven; the thesis at the beginning of the essay felt merely claimed.
Synthesis of Main Points
Briefly synthesise (not list) the main arguments made in the body. This should demonstrate how the evidence collectively supports the thesis — not simply repeat what each paragraph said. One or two sentences per main point, drawing them together into a coherent summary.
Broader Significance or Closing Thought
End with a statement that places the argument in a broader context: implications for policy, society, or further research; a call to action; or a final thought that leaves the reader with something to consider. Avoid introducing new arguments or new evidence in the conclusion. Do not end with "In conclusion, I have shown that..." — the conclusion should show, not announce (Wingate, 2019, p. 110).
Section VI

Revision and Editing Strategies

Writing is rewriting. First drafts are never final drafts — they are the material from which a finished essay is shaped through deliberate revision (Murray & Hughes, 2019, p. 78). Effective academic writers distinguish between two stages: revision (addressing large-scale issues of argument, structure, and content) and editing (addressing sentence-level issues of grammar, punctuation, and style).

LevelWhat to CheckKey Questions to Ask
Revision (Global)Thesis, argument, structure, evidenceDoes every paragraph serve the thesis? Is the argument logical and progressive? Is the evidence sufficient and relevant? Are counterarguments acknowledged?
Paragraph LevelPEEL structure, topic sentences, transitionsDoes each paragraph have a clear point? Is evidence followed by explanation? Are paragraphs connected by transitions?
Sentence LevelClarity, concision, varietyIs each sentence clear and direct? Are there unnecessary words? Is there variety in sentence length and structure?
Editing (Surface)Grammar, spelling, punctuation, citationsAre there subject-verb agreement errors? Are all sources cited correctly in APA format? Are apostrophes used correctly?
📌

The Read-Aloud Method: Reading your essay aloud is one of the most effective editing tools available. Your ear will catch awkward constructions, missing words, unclear sentences, and tonal inconsistencies that your eye skips over when reading silently. If you stumble over a sentence while reading it aloud, rewrite it until it flows naturally (Murray & Hughes, 2019, p. 85).

Section VII

Practical Writing Activity

🖌
Practical Activity — Section VII
Essay Writing II Exercises

Apply the advanced essay writing techniques from this lecture to the following exercises. All written work should aim for academic register and precision.

  • 1Convert this weak thesis into a strong, specific, arguable thesis statement: "The internet has affected education in Nigeria." Write three different strong versions and explain what makes each one more effective.
  • 2Write a complete PEEL paragraph on the following topic sentence: "The lack of consistent electricity supply in Nigerian universities significantly hinders effective digital learning." Your evidence must be cited from a credible source.
  • 3The following paragraph has no transitional expressions. Rewrite it, adding appropriate transitions, and explain why you chose each one: "Universities in Nigeria face funding challenges. Lecturers often leave for better-paying positions abroad. Students sometimes lack access to up-to-date textbooks. Research output is lower than in comparable institutions in other African countries."
  • 4Practise source integration. Take the following quotation and: (a) use it as a direct quotation, (b) paraphrase it, and (c) summarise it. Cite each version correctly in APA 7th edition: "Academic literacy is not merely a technical skill but a social practice that differs across disciplines, institutions, and cultures." (Wingate, 2019, p. 88)
  • 5Write a complete introduction (120–150 words) for an essay arguing that "Nigerian universities should adopt a four-day teaching week to improve lecturer productivity and student wellbeing." Your introduction must include a hook, contextual background, and a clear thesis statement.
  • 6Write a conclusion (120–150 words) for the same essay topic above. Your conclusion must restate the thesis in different words, synthesise the main points of the essay (invent two or three plausible arguments for the purpose of this exercise), and end with a statement of broader significance.
  • 7Read the following body paragraph and identify all the problems at the revision and editing levels. Then rewrite it to correct them: "There is problems in Nigerian schools. Students they do not have books. The government should do something. Many studies shows this is a big issue. Education is important for development of any country."
  • 8Apply the four-level revision checklist to one of your own previous essays or any 500-word writing sample. Identify at least three issues at the global level (argument/structure), three at the paragraph level, and three at the sentence/editing level. Rewrite the passage applying all corrections.
  • 9Write a 400–500 word essay in response to the following prompt: "University education in Nigeria should be free for all qualified students." Apply every technique covered in Lectures 4.1 and 4.2: planning, strong thesis, PEEL paragraphs, transitions, cited evidence, effective introduction and conclusion.
  • 10Exchange your essay from Activity 9 with a classmate. Using the four-level revision checklist from Section VI, provide written feedback on their essay. Your feedback must be specific and constructive — identify what works well and what needs improvement, with reasons for each observation.
Conclusion

Key Takeaway

Advanced essay writing is not simply about following rules — it is about developing a set of disciplined intellectual habits: the habit of constructing a precise, arguable thesis; the habit of building evidence-led, analytically rich paragraphs; the habit of connecting ideas cohesively; the habit of integrating sources while maintaining your own academic voice; and the habit of revising your work with critical detachment (Lunsford, 2021, p. 88).


Together, Lectures 4.1 and 4.2 provide the full framework for academic essay writing at university level. The skills developed here — planning, structuring, arguing, evidencing, and revising — are transferable across all disciplines and all genres of academic writing. Lecture 4.3 applies these skills to a specific and practical genre: letter writing, both formal and informal, in Nigerian and international contexts (Andrews & Smith, 2018, p. 100).
